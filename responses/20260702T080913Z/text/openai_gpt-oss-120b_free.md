### 1. Understanding the Grant‑Seeking Landscape  

| Aspect | What It Means for You | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Funding Types** | Federal, state, corporate, foundation, community‑based, donor‑advised funds | Each has its own cycles, eligibility, and reporting requirements |
| **Grant Size** | Micro‑grants ($500‑$5,000) → Major grants ($100K‑$5M) | Aligns with your project scope and capacity |
| **Funding Priorities** | Education, health, climate, equity, arts, etc. | Matching priorities increases success odds |
| **Application Formats** | Online portals (e.g., Grants.gov), PDFs, narrative letters | Knowing the format avoids last‑minute technical glitches |
| **Decision Timelines** | 30‑90 days (quick‑turn) → 6‑12 months (large foundations) | Plan your program calendar accordingly |

### 2. Researching Funders – Who to Target & How  

**Step‑by‑Step Research Workflow**  

1. **Define Your Project Parameters**  
   - Goal, target population, geographic area, timeline, budget range.  

2. **Create a Master Funder List** *(Spreadsheet columns shown below)*  

| Funder Name | Type | Funding Priorities | Typical Award Size | Deadline(s) | Application URL | Contact | Notes |
|------------|------|-------------------|--------------------|-------------|-----------------|---------|-------|
| Example Foundation | Private | Youth mental health | $25‑$100K | Feb 15, Sep 15 | link | Jane Doe, Program Officer | Requires site visit |

3. **Use Multiple Research Sources**  

| Source | How to Use | Tips |
|--------|------------|------|
| Foundation Directory Online (FDO) | Keyword search + filter by geography | Export results to CSV |
| GuideStar / Candid | Look at past grantees, grant amounts | Check “Recent Grants” tab |
| Grants.gov | Federal opportunities | Set up email alerts |
| Local Community Foundations | Often have “rapid response” funds | Attend their informational webinars |
| IRS 990s of Peer Orgs | Identify who else receives money | Look for “Grantor” line items |

4. **Score Each Funder** (1‑5 scale)  

| Criterion | Weight | Score | Weighted Total |
|-----------|--------|-------|----------------|
| Alignment with mission | 30% | 4 | 1.2 |
| Fit of award size | 20% | 5 | 1.0 |
| Application complexity | 15% | 3 | 0.45 |
| Reporting burden | 15% | 2 | 0.30 |
| Relationship potential | 20% | 4 | 0.8 |
| **Total** | 100% |   | **3.75** |

5. **Prioritize** – Focus on funders scoring **≥3.5** and create a “top‑10” target list.  

**Concrete Example**  

> *Your nonprofit “Healthy Futures” wants to launch a mobile dental clinic for low‑income seniors in Rural County.*  
> After scoring, you identify:  
> - Rural Health Foundation (Score 4.2) – $75K, deadline Aug 1.  
> - State Dept. of Health (Score 3.9) – $150K, deadline Oct 15.  
> - Community Trust (Score 3.6) – $30K, rolling deadline.

### 3. Building a Strong Organizational Profile  

| Element | What to Include | How to Make It Persuasive |
|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Mission & Vision** | One‑sentence mission + 2‑sentence vision | Use language that mirrors funder’s priorities |
| **History & Impact** | Year founded, key milestones, quantitative outcomes | Highlight numbers (e.g., “served 12,000 patients in 2023”) |
| **Leadership & Governance** | Board bios, staff qualifications, turnover rate | Show stability & expertise |
| **Financial Health** | FY‑end financial statements, audited if possible, revenue mix | Include a 3‑year trend chart |
| **Programmatic Capacity** | Number of staff, volunteers, partners, facilities | Demonstrate ability to manage the grant |
| **Strategic Alignment** | How the proposed project fits into your 3‑year plan | Tie directly to your own strategic goals |

**Checklist – Organizational Profile Package**  

- [ ] Mission & Vision statement (≤ 2 sentences)  
- [ ] 1‑page “Impact Snapshot” (infographic)  
- [ ] Current audited financial statements (or most recent review)  
- [ ] Org chart with key staff for the project  
- [ ] Letters of support from two strategic partners  
- [ ] IRS 501(c)(3) determination letter (copy)

### 4. Crafting a Compelling Needs Statement  

**Formula: Situation → Gap → Consequence → Solution (Your Project)**  

1. **Situation** – Set the context with data.  
   - *Example:* “In Rural County, 38% of seniors lack regular dental care, double the state average (County Health Dept., 2024).”  

2. **Gap** – Identify what is missing.  
   - *Example:* “Only one stationary clinic operates 20 miles away, creating transportation barriers.”  

3. **Consequence** – Explain why the gap matters.  
   - *Example:* “Untreated oral disease leads to malnutrition, increased hospitalizations, and an estimated $2.3 M in avoidable health costs annually.”  

4. **Solution** – Briefly preview your project.  
   - *Example:* “A mobile dental unit will bring preventive and restorative services directly to seniors’ neighborhoods, reducing emergency visits by 25% within two years.”  

**Tips for a Powerful Narrative**  

- Use **local, recent statistics** (last 3 years).  
- Quote **one credible community voice** (e.g., a senior center director).  
- Keep the paragraph **150‑250 words** – concise but vivid.  

**Sample Needs Statement (Full Paragraph)**  

> “Rural County’s aging population faces a silent crisis: 38 % of adults over 65 have not visited a dentist in the past year, a rate that is twice the state average (County Health Dept., 2024). The sole fixed‑site clinic is located 20 miles from the county’s largest senior housing complex, and limited public transit makes routine appointments impractical. As a result, untreated dental disease contributes to malnutrition, exacerbates chronic conditions such as diabetes, and drives an estimated $2.3 million in avoidable emergency medical costs each year (Health Economics Review, 2023). A mobile dental unit, staffed by licensed hygienists and a rotating dentist, will bring preventive screenings, cleanings, and basic restorative care directly to seniors where they live, aiming to reduce emergency dental visits by 25 % within the first two years of operation.”

### 5. Designing a Logic Model that Speaks Funders’ Language  

**Standard Logic Model Components**  

| Component | Definition | Example (Mobile Dental Project) |
|-----------|------------|---------------------------------|
| **Inputs** | Resources you will use | Grant funds, mobile van, staff, dental supplies |
| **Activities** | What you will do | Schedule weekly visits, conduct screenings, provide cleanings |
| **Outputs** | Direct deliverables | # of sites visited, # of patients screened, # of cleanings performed |
| **Outcomes – Short Term** | Immediate changes | Increased oral health knowledge, identified treatment needs |
| **Outcomes – Intermediate** | Behavioral/condition changes | Reduced untreated decay, increased regular dental visits |
| **Impact** | Long‑term community effect | Lower health‑care costs, improved overall senior health |

**Visual Layout (ASCII)**  

```
Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Short‑Term Outcomes → Intermediate Outcomes → Impact
```

**How to Tailor for Funders**  

- **Match terminology** in the funder’s RFP (e.g., “capacity building,” “community resilience”).  
- **Quantify** each output and outcome (e.g., “300 seniors screened per quarter”).  
- **Link to metrics** the funder already tracks (e.g., “reduce emergency department visits for dental issues by 10%”).  

**Logic Model Checklist**  

- [ ] All six components present  
- [ ] Each component has at least one measurable indicator  
- [ ] Data sources identified for each indicator (e.g., electronic health records, survey)  
- [ ] Timeline assigned (Quarter 1, Q2, …)  
- [ ] Alignment with funder’s stated outcomes

### 6. Developing a Realistic, Transparent Budget  

**Budget Categories (Common Funders)**  

| Category | Typical Sub‑items | Example Amount (USD) |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Personnel | Salaries, fringe benefits, contractor fees | $45,000 |
| Travel | Mileage, fuel, vehicle maintenance | $5,200 |
| Equipment | Mobile van purchase/lease, dental chair | $80,000 |
| Supplies | Gloves, dental materials, educational pamphlets | $12,000 |
| Indirect Costs | Overhead, admin (often capped at 10 %) | $9,600 |
| Evaluation | Data collection tools, analyst time | $7,500 |
| **Total Direct** | – | **$149,700** |
| **Indirect (10 %)** | – | **$14,970** |
| **Grand Total** | – | **$164,670** |

**Budget Narrative Tips**  

1. **Justify Every Line** – Explain *why* each cost is essential.  
2. **Show Cost‑Sharing** – If you are contributing in‑kind (e.g., staff time), note it.  
3. **Stay Within Limits** – Some funders cap equipment at 30 % of total; ensure compliance.  
4. **Round Numbers** – Use whole dollars; avoid “$0.00” entries.  

**Sample Budget Narrative (Personnel)**  

> “Project Coordinator (0.75 FTE) – $30,000 salary + 20 % benefits = $36,000. The Coordinator will oversee site scheduling, community outreach, and data reporting. The 0.75 FTE reflects the anticipated 30 hours/week commitment, allowing the Coordinator to maintain existing program duties while leading the mobile unit.”

### 7. Writing the Narrative – Structure & Style Tips  

**Typical Narrative Sections (in order)**  

1. **Executive Summary** (½ page) – Snapshot of need, solution, budget, and requested amount.  
2. **Organizational Background** (1 page) – Mission, capacity, past successes.  
3. **Problem/Needs Statement** (1–2 pages) – Data‑driven, human‑centered story.  
4. **Project Description**  
   - Goals & Objectives (SMART)  
   - Methods/Activities  
   - Timeline (Gantt or month‑by‑month)  
   - Logic Model (appendix)  
5. **Evaluation Plan** – Indicators, data sources, analysis, reporting schedule.  
6. **Sustainability** – How the program will continue after grant ends.  
7. **Budget Summary** – Table + narrative.  
8. **Appendices** – Letters of support, IRS letter, board roster, etc.  

**Style Checklist**  

- **Active voice** (“We will deliver”)  
- **Past tense for track record** (“We served 5,000 youth in 2023”)  
- **Future tense for proposed work** (“We will train”)  
- **Avoid jargon** – Explain “case management” if not universal.  
- **Consistent terminology** – Use the same phrase for the project name throughout.  
- **Word limit compliance** – Use a word‑count tool before finalizing.  

**Example SMART Objective**  

> “By month 12, the mobile dental unit will provide preventive screenings to at least 500 seniors, achieving a 25 % reduction in untreated decay among participants as measured by baseline and follow‑up oral health assessments.”

### 8. Assembling Supporting Materials  

| Material | Why It’s Needed | Common Pitfalls | Quick Fix |
|----------|----------------|----------------|----------|
| IRS 501(c)(3) Determination Letter | Proof of nonprofit status | Scanned copy is blurry | Use high‑resolution PDF |

**Packaging Tip**  

- **Create a “Grant Package Master Folder”** on your shared drive with sub‑folders: `01_Application`, `02_Budget`, `03_SupportDocs`.  
- Name each file with the funder’s acronym and content (e.g., `RHF_Budget_2024.pdf`).

### 9. The Review & Edit Cycle  

1. **First Draft** – Writer focuses on content, not perfection.  
2. **Peer Review** – Two staff members (one not involved in writing) read for clarity & logic.  
3. **Subject‑Matter Expert (SME) Review** – Program director checks technical accuracy.  
4. **Finance Review** – Grant manager verifies budget alignment.  
5. **Executive Review** – Executive Director signs off on overall fit and compliance.  
6. **Proofreading** – Professional editor or dedicated staff checks grammar, spelling, and formatting.  

**Editing Checklist (20 items)**  

- [ ] All page limits respected  
- [ ] Font size & style consistent (e.g., Times New Roman 12 pt)  
- [ ] Margins 1” all sides  
- [ ] Page numbers present  
- [ ] Section headings match Table of Contents  
- [ ] No “TK” placeholders left  
- [ ] All acronyms spelled out on first use  
- [ ] Numbers under 10 written out; larger numbers use digits  
- [ ] Dollar amounts formatted as $XX,XXX  
- [ ] Dates in “Month Day, Year” format  
- [ ] All citations include source & year  
- [ ] No duplicated content across sections  
- [ ] Figures/tables labeled and referenced in text  
- [ ] All attachments listed in “Attachments” section  
- [ ] File names follow funder’s naming convention  
- [ ] PDFs are not password‑protected (unless required)  
- [ ] Final file size < funder’s limit (often 10 MB)  
- [ ] Accessibility check (alt‑text for images)  
- [ ] Spell‑check completed in the final PDF version  
- [ ] Confirmation email drafted for submission

### 10. Submitting the Application – Checklist & Timing  

| Task | Deadline (relative) | Owner | Status |
|------|---------------------|-------|--------|
| Final internal approval | T‑14 days | Exec Dir | ☐ |
| Upload to portal (test) | T‑7 days | Grant Manager | ☐ |
| Verify required fields (mandatory) | T‑5 days | Grant Writer | ☐ |
| Attach all supporting docs | T‑3 days | Admin Assistant | ☐ |
| Run final PDF integrity check | T‑2 days | IT Support | ☐ |
| Submit application | T‑0 (deadline day) | Grant Manager | ☐ |
| Receive confirmation receipt | T + 0‑1 day | Grant Manager | ☐ |
| Save copy to organizational archive | T + 1 day | Records Officer | ☐ |

**Timing Tips**  

- **Set internal deadline at least 48 hours before the official deadline.**  
- **If the portal closes at 5 pm EST, aim to submit by 2 pm EST** to allow for unexpected glitches.  
- **Keep a “submission screenshot”** as proof of on‑time delivery.

### 11. Post‑Submission Follow‑Up  

- **Thank‑you email** to the program officer (within 24 hrs).  
- **Track status** in a spreadsheet (columns: Funder, Submission Date, Confirmation #, Decision Date, Outcome).  
- **If rejected**, request feedback (most funders provide brief rationale).  

**Re‑submission Planning**  

- Update the logic model with any new data.  
- Adjust budget based on feedback (e.g., reduce indirect cost rate).  
- Keep the original proposal in a “Rejected – 2024” folder for reference.

### 12. Award Acceptance & Post‑Award Reporting  

#### 12.1 Acceptance  

1. **Sign the Grant Agreement** – Verify award amount, reporting schedule, and any special conditions (e.g., audit rights).  
2. **Enter award into financial system** – Create a separate fund code (e.g., `RHF2024‑MOBILE`).  
3. **Notify staff** – Email “Kick‑off” memo with key dates, deliverables, and point‑people.  

#### 12.2 Implementation Tracking  

| Metric | Frequency | Data Source | Person Responsible |
|--------|-----------|-------------|--------------------|
| Sites visited | Monthly | Field logs | Project Coordinator |
| Patients screened | Monthly | EMR export | Data Analyst |
| Supplies used | Quarterly | Inventory log | Procurement Officer |
| Budget spend | Quarterly | Accounting software | Finance Manager |

#### 12.3 Reporting Requirements  

| Report Type | Due | Content | Length |
|-------------|-----|---------|--------|
| Quarterly Narrative | 30 days after quarter end | Progress vs. objectives, challenges, adjustments | ≤ 2 pages |
| Financial Report | Same as narrative | Expenditures by budget line, variance analysis | Standard template |
| Final Impact Report | 90 days after project end | Outcomes, lessons learned, sustainability plan | 5‑7 pages + annexes |
| Site Visit (if required) | As scheduled | Presentation of data, Q&A | 30‑minute oral brief |

**Reporting Checklist**  

- [ ] All required forms downloaded from funder portal  
- [ ] Data validated (cross‑checked with source systems)  
- [ ] Narrative follows funder’s word limit & headings  
- [ ] Attach required photos (if stipulated)  
- [ ] Submit via required method (email, portal upload)  
- [ ] Archive a copy in `01_AwardManagement/Reports`  

#### 12.4 Sustaining the Relationship  

- Send a **mid‑project “impact snapshot”** (infographic) to the funder.  
- Invite the program officer to **visit a service site**.  
- Share **press releases** or **social media posts** highlighting the partnership (with funder’s permission).

### 13. Continuous Learning & Grant Management System  

| Action | Frequency | Tool | Owner |
|--------|-----------|------|-------|

| Staff grant‑writing training | Semi‑annually | In‑house workshop or external trainer | HR & Development |

**Key Performance Indicators for Your Grant Office**  

- **Application Success Rate** – # awards ÷ # submissions (target ≥30 %).  
- **Average Funding Amount** – total awarded ÷ # awards (track growth).  
- **Reporting Compliance** – % of reports submitted on time (target 100 %).  
- **Donor Retention** – % of funders giving in consecutive years (target ≥60 %).

## Quick‑Start Checklist for a New Grant Application  

1. **Define Project** – goal, target, budget range.  
2. **Research Funders** – compile master list, score, select top 3‑5.  
3. **Gather Org Docs** – IRS letter, latest audit, board list.  
4. **Write Needs Statement** – data + human story.  
5. **Build Logic Model** – link inputs → impact.  
6. **Draft Budget** – line‑item + narrative.  
7. **Write Narrative** – follow funder’s template.  
8. **Secure Support Letters** – ask partners 2 weeks early.  
9. **Internal Review Cycle** – writer → peer → SME → finance → exec.  
10. **Finalize Files** – PDF, correct naming, size < limit.  
11. **Submit** – 48 hrs before deadline; capture receipt.  
12. **Send Thank‑You** – email program officer.  
13. **Record Award** – fund code, calendar of reports.  
14. **Implement & Track** – monthly data collection.  
15. **Report & Celebrate** – on‑time reports, share outcomes.
