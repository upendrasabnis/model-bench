### 1. Researching Funders  

8. **1.1 Identify Mission Alignment** – Match your program’s core purpose with the funder’s stated mission.  
9. **1.2 Map Recent Grants** – Search the funder’s database for the last 3‑5 years of awarded grants.  
10. **1.3 Note Funding Priorities** – Look for recurring themes (e.g., “youth employment,” “environmental justice”).  
11. **1.4 Check Eligibility Criteria** – Verify geographic, size‑of‑budget, and project‑type restrictions.  
12. **1.5 Review Application Guides** – Read RFPs, FAQs, and past award announcements carefully.  
13. **1.6 Create a Funder Scorecard** – Rate each funder on alignment, competitiveness, and fit (1‑5 scale).  
14. **1.7 Build a Funder Contact List** – Record program officers, email addresses, and preferred communication style.  
15. **1.8 Conduct Informational Interviews** – Ask 2‑3 targeted questions about upcoming cycles.  
16. **1.9 Track Submission Deadlines** – Use a shared calendar with alerts 30, 15, and 7 days before due dates.  
17. **1.10 Example:** *The XYZ Foundation funds “STEM after‑school programs in low‑income zip codes.”*  
18. **1.11 Checklist – Funder Research**  
19. **   • Mission match?**  
20. **   • Recent grant size range?**  
21. **   • Geographic focus?**  
22. **   • Reporting requirements?**  
23. **   • Contact person & timeline?**

### 2. Understanding the Funder’s Review Process  

24. **2.1 Learn Scoring Rubrics** – Many funders publish criteria such as “Impact, Feasibility, Sustainability.”  
25. **2.2 Identify Review Panel Composition** – Note whether reviewers are practitioners, academics, or staff.  
26. **2.3 Determine Weighting** – Some funders weight “need” higher than “budget realism.”  
27. **2.4 Prepare for Peer Review** – Anticipate questions about methodology and community engagement.  
28. **2.5 Example:** *A foundation uses a 60‑point rubric: 30 pts need, 20 pts approach, 10 pts capacity, 20 pts budget, 20 pts sustainability.*  
29. **2.6 Checklist – Review Process Insight**  
30. **   • Scoring categories defined?**  
31. **   • Reviewer expertise known?**  
32. **   • Decision timeline clear?**  
33. **   • Opportunity for pre‑submission Q&A?**

### 3. Crafting a Compelling Needs Statement  

34. **3.1 Start with a Data Snapshot** – Use census, school, or health department statistics.  
35. **3.2 Quantify the Gap** – Show the difference between current services and optimal outcomes.  
36. **3.3 Humanize with a Story** – Include a brief anecdote of a community member affected.  
37. **3.4 Cite Credible Sources** – Government reports, peer‑reviewed studies, or reputable NGOs.  
38. **3.5 Use “Problem–Impact–Urgency” Structure**  
   38a. **Problem:** *30 % of youth in County X lack after‑school tutoring.*  
   38b. **Impact:** *Resulting in a 15 % decline in graduation rates.*  
   38c. **Urgency:** *Without intervention, the trend will cost the community $2 M in lost earnings.*  
39. **3.6 Align with Funder Priorities** – Mirror the funder’s language (e.g., “capacity building”).  
40. **3.7 Example Paragraph:**  
   > “In 2023, the City Y Health Department reported that 4,200 children under 12 missed at least one wellness visit, leading to a 12 % increase in preventable hospitalizations. Families in the Eastside neighborhood cite transportation barriers and lack of affordable childcare as primary obstacles. Immediate action is required to prevent a projected 20 % rise in emergency room visits by 2026.”  
41. **3.8 Checklist – Needs Statement**  
42. **   • Data‑driven statistics included?**  
43. **   • Clear description of the gap?**  
44. **   • Community voice represented?**  
45. **   • Impact quantified?**  
46. **   • Urgency conveyed?**  
47. **   • Language mirrors funder’s priorities?**

### 4. Defining Goals, Objectives, and Outcomes  

48. **4.1 Distinguish Goal vs. Objective**  
   48a. **Goal:** Broad, long‑term change (e.g., “Improve youth literacy”).  
   48b. **Objective:** Specific, measurable target (e.g., “Increase reading scores by 10 % for 200 participants”).  
49. **4.2 Apply SMART Criteria** – Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time‑bound.  
50. **4.3 Link Each Objective to an Outcome** – Outcome = the benefit to the target population.  
51. **4.4 Example Logic Chain:**  
   51a. **Goal:** Reduce food insecurity among seniors.  
   51b. **Objective 1:** Deliver 1,200 meal kits per month.  
   51c. **Outcome 1:** 80 % of recipients report improved nutritional intake.  
   51d. **Objective 2:** Conduct 12 nutrition workshops.  
   51e. **Outcome 2:** 70 % of participants adopt healthier cooking habits.  
52. **4.5 Example Objective Statement:**  
   > “By December 2025, the program will increase the proportion of participating households that consume a balanced dinner at least five times per week from 45 % to 70 %.”  
53. **4.6 Checklist – Goals & Objectives**  
54. **   • Goal aligns with funder’s mission?**  
55. **   • Objectives are SMART?**  
56. **   • Each objective has a clear outcome?**  
57. **   • Outcomes are measurable and attributable?**  
58. **   • Timeframe realistic?**

### 5. Designing a Logic Model  

59. **5.1 Core Components** – Inputs, Activities, Outputs, Outcomes, Impacts.  
60. **5.2 Visual Layout** – Use a left‑to‑right flowchart or a table.  
61. **5.3 Input Example:** *Funding of $150,000, Staff (Program Manager, 2 Facilitators), Partnerships with 3 schools.*  
62. **5.4 Activity Example:** *Weekly after‑school tutoring sessions.*  
63. **5.5 Output Example:** *300 tutoring hours delivered.*  
64. **5.6 Short‑Term Outcome Example:** *80 % of participants improve test scores.*  
65. **5.7 Mid‑Term Outcome Example:** *40 % increase in school attendance.*  
66. **5.8 Long‑Term Impact Example:** *Higher graduation rates for at‑risk youth.*  
67. **5.9 Example Logic Model Table:**  

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Inputs** | $150k grant, 3 staff, 5 partner sites |

| **Outcomes (1‑yr)** | 80 % reading gain, 70 % parent engagement |

68. **5.10 Checklist – Logic Model**  
69. **   • All components clearly labeled?**  
70. **   • Inputs realistic and justified?**  
71. **   • Activities directly linked to outputs?**

### 6. Writing a Persuasive Project Description  

74. **6.1 Executive Summary (1‑2 paragraphs)** – Summarize need, solution, and expected impact.  
75. **6.2 Problem Statement (expanded)** – Build on the needs statement with deeper evidence.  
76. **6.3 Solution Overview** – Describe the program design in plain language.  
77. **6.4 Implementation Plan** – Outline phases, timelines, and responsible parties.  
78. **6.5 Staffing & Partnerships** – Highlight key personnel and collaborator expertise.  
79. **6.6 Sustainability Strategy** – Explain how the program will continue after grant period.  
80. **6.7 Example Paragraph:**  
   > “The ‘Read‑Rise’ initiative will deploy a team of two certified literacy coaches to deliver weekly small‑group sessions for 200 elementary students in underserved neighborhoods. Leveraging a partnership with the local library system, the program will integrate interactive reading games and parental literacy workshops, aiming to raise reading proficiency by 10 % within one academic year.”  
81. **6.8 Checklist – Project Description**  
82. **   • Executive summary compelling and concise?**  
83. **   • Problem statement data‑rich and focused?**  
84. **   • Solution clearly described?**  
85. **   • Implementation timeline realistic?**  
86. **   • Staffing plan demonstrates capacity?**  
87. **   • Sustainability plan credible?**

### 7. Developing a Detailed Budget  

88. **7.1 Budget Categories** – Personnel, Fringe Benefits, Sub‑grants, Equipment, Supplies, Travel, Indirect Costs.  
89. **7.2 Personnel Detail** – Include job titles, FTE (Full‑Time Equivalent), hourly rate or salary, and effort percentage.  
90. **7.3 Example Personnel Line:**  
   > “Program Manager – 0.5 FTE – $70,000 annual salary – $35,000 (50 % of salary).”  
91. **7.4 Fringe Benefits** – Calculate at the organization’s negotiated rate (e.g., 30 % of salaries).  
92. **7.5 Sub‑grant Costs** – List partner agency budgets, ensuring they are reasonable and necessary.  
93. **7.6 Equipment vs. Supplies** – Equipment = items with a useful life > 1 year; Supplies = consumables.  
94. **7.7 Travel Costs** – Specify purpose, destination, and per‑diem rates.  
95. **7.8 Indirect Costs** – Apply the negotiated indirect cost rate (e.g., 10 %).  
96. **7.9 Example Budget Summary Table:**  

| Category | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Personnel | $120,000 |
| Fringe Benefits | $36,000 |
| Sub‑grants | $30,000 |
| Equipment | $5,000 |
| Supplies | $4,000 |
| Travel | $3,000 |
| Indirect Costs (10 %) | $19,800 |
| **Total Request** | **$217,800** |

97. **7.10 Budget Narrative Tips** – Explain *why* each line is essential; tie back to objectives.  
98. **7.11 Common Budget Errors** – Over‑ or under‑estimating, double‑counting, missing indirect costs.  
99. **7.12 Checklist – Budget**  
100. **   • All cost categories accounted for?**  
101. **   • Rates consistent with organizational policies?**  
102. **   • Narrative justifies each expense?**  
103. **   • Total does not exceed funder’s limit?**  
104. **   • Budget format matches funder’s template?**

### 8. Aligning Language with Funder Priorities  

105. **8.1 Use Funder‑Specific Keywords** – Mirror terms from the RFP (e.g., “capacity building,” “evidence‑based”).  
106. **8.2 Avoid Jargon** – Write for a non‑technical audience; define acronyms on first use.  
107. **8.3 Emphasize Equity & Inclusion** – Show how the project addresses disparities.  
108. **8.4 Example Phrase:**  
   > “The project will prioritize culturally responsive pedagogy to serve English‑language learners.”  
109. **8.5 Checklist – Language Alignment**  
110. **   • Keywords from RFP woven throughout?**  
111. **   • Tone matches funder’s style (e.g., collaborative vs. competitive)?**  
112. **   • Equity considerations explicitly addressed?**

120. **9.8 Checklist – Narrative Draft**  
121. **   • Hook grabs attention?**  
122. **   • Need clearly articulated with data?**  
123. **   • Solution logically follows need?**  
124. **   • Capacity demonstrated convincingly?**

138. **12.1 Acceptance Letter** – Respond promptly, confirming receipt and next steps.  
139. **12.2 Develop a Grant Management Plan** – Outline reporting schedule, data collection, and point of contact.  
140. **12.3 Set Up Financial Tracking** – Use a separate cost‑center or project code in accounting software.

### 14. Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them  

155. **14.1 “One‑Size‑Fits‑All” Proposals** – Tailor each application to the specific funder.  
156. **14.2 Over‑Promising** – Set realistic objectives; under‑promising can be corrected later.  
157. **14.3 Ignoring Budget Constraints** – Align requested amount with demonstrated need.  
158. **14.4 Weak Logic Model** – Ensure each activity logically leads to the intended outcome.  
159. **14.5 Missing Attachments** – Use a pre‑submission checklist; double‑check before hitting “Submit.”
