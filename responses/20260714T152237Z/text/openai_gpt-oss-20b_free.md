## 1. Why Grant Writing Matters  

Grant money can be a **seasonal lifeline** for many nonprofits, often filling gaps that member dues, events, or individual gifts cannot. However, grant writing is **not a quick fix**; it’s a strategic activity that requires:

- **Deep understanding** of the issue you’re solving.  
- **Clear evidence** that your organization can deliver results.  
- **Robust accountability** to funders, partners, and the public.  

> *Bottom line:* A well‑written grant proposal can mean the difference between a program’s survival or its closure. Master the craft and secure the future.

### 3.3 Develop a “Fit Analysis”  

> **Fit** is a negotiation timestam.  
> The higher the fit score, the more likely you’ll win.

**Fit Components**  
1. **Mission alignment** – Does the funder’s goal match yours?  
2. **Programmatic alignment** – Does the focus area match?  
3. **Geographic alignment** – Are you in the same region?  
4. **Capacity alignment** – Does the funder fund organizations of your size?  
5. **Administrative alignment** – Does the funder accept online applications or require hard copies?

Score each 1–5 and sum. A **score of 18+** is a strong match.

## 5. Crafting a Compelling Needs Statement  

### 5.1 The Classic “Problem – Evidence – Impact” Structure  

1. **Problem** – What is the issue?  
2. **Evidence** – Data, anecdotes, and research.  
3. **Impact** – Who is affected and how?

### 5.2 Example Needs Statement  

> **Problem:** “In our city, 35% of high‑school graduates are homeless upon graduation.”  
> **Evidence:** “A 2023 city report shows 1, निध 2,000 youths living on streets.”  
> **Impact:** “These youths face chronic health problems, limited employment, and a 70% dropout rate from post‑secondary education.”

### 5.3 Tips  

- **Keep it concise** – 2–3 sentences.  
- **Use a narrative voice** – “When Maria left home at 16…”  
- **Quantify when possible** – “X% of Y.”  
- **Show urgency** – “The window to intervene is 24 hrs.”

## 6. Designing the Program (Logic Models)  

### 6.1 What is a Logic Model?  

A visual representation that links:

- **Inputs** – resources, staff, materials.  
- **Activities** – what you do.  
- **Outputs** – tangible deliverables.  
- **Outcomes** – short‑term changes.  
- **Impact** – long‑term societal benefits.

### 6.2 Building a Logic Model – Step‑by‑Step  

1. **Identify Inputs** – e.g., 4 staff, $10k equipment, 50 volunteers.  
2. **Define Activities Admissions** – e.g., “Monthly counseling sessions.”  
3. **Specify Outputs** – e.g., “120 counseling sessions delivered.”  
4. **Set Outcomes** – e.g., “30% of participants report reduced anxiety.”  
5. **State Impact** – e.g., “Participants maintain stable housing 2 years post‑program.”

### 6.3 Example Logic Model (Text)  

| **Inputs** | **Activities** | **Outputs** | **Outcomes** | **Impact** |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|--------------|------------|
| 4 staff, $15k | 1) Intake assessments, 2) Weekly group therapy | 200 assessments, 80 therapy sessions | 25% improvement in mental health scores | Reduced homelessness by 15% over 3 years |
| 10 volunteers | rebuilt community garden | 1 garden,-display of produce | 30% increase in local food access | Improved nutrition in low‑income families |

### 6.4 Tips  

- **Align the logic model with the grant’s evaluation criteria**.  
- **Keep it simple** – 1–2 pages or a 1‑slide diagram.  
- **Use measurable indicators** – avoid vague terms like “improve.”

## 7. Writing the Narrative  

### 7.1 The “Problem – Solution – Impact” Template  

1. **Problem** – Reiterate the needs statement.  
2. **Solution** – Describe your program, its activities, and the logic model.  
3. **Impact** – Quantify expected results, using past data or pilot evidence.

### 7.2 Narrative Flow  

| Section | Length | Key Elements | Tips |
|---------|--------|--------------|------|
| **Cover Letter** | 1 page | Name, request, quick hook | Personalize each letter |
| **Executive Summary** | 1Ω page | Problem, solution, budget, call to attitude | 1‑2 sentences each |
| **Program Narrative** | 4–6 pages | Needs, goals, activities, logic model | Use bullet points for clarity |
| **Staff & Management** | 1 page | Bios, roles, capacity | Emphasize experience |
| **Evaluation Plan** | 1 page | Methods, indicators, frequency | Align with funder metrics |
| **Sustainability** | ½ page | Post‑grant plan | Show long‑term vision |
| **Budget Narrative** | ½ page | Justify major line items | Tie to program activities |
| **Conclusion** | ½ page | Recap, call to partnership | End with a strong statement |

### 7.3 Tone & Style  

- **Clear, concise, and jargon‑free.**  
- **Humanize data** – stories of individuals impacted.  
- **Active voice** – “We will” not “It will be.”  
- **Consistent formatting** – margins, fonts, headings.  

### 7.4 Example Excerpt (Problem–Solution–Impact)  

> **Problem:** “Youth homelessness in Cityrichten is rising, with 1,200 young adults living on streets.”  
> **Solution:** “Our program offers a 12‑month mentorship and transitional housing model, proven to reduce homelessness by 40% in pilot studies.”  
> **Impact:** “We anticipate that 80 participants will secure stable housing and employment, contributing $2M in economic activity over the next two years.”

## 8. Budgeting  

### 8.1 Types of Costs  

| Category | Description | Example Items |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Personnel** | Salaries, benefits | Program Manager, Social Worker |
| **Direct Costs** | Materials, equipment, services | Counseling supplies, rent |
| **Indirect Costs** | Overhead, admin | Office rent, utilities |
| **Capital Costs** | One‑time purchases | Computers, vehicles hookup |
| **Other** | Travel, training | Conferences, staff training |

### 8.2 Budget Line‑Item Example  

| Line | Title | Amount | Justification |
|------|-------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | Program Manager Salary | $42,000 | Full‑time (50% FTE) |
| 2 | Social Worker Salary | $36,000 | Full‑time (50% FTE) |
| 3 | Rent (Program Space) | $8,400 | $700/month for 12 months |
| 4 | Counseling Supplies | $2,000 | Materials, worksheets, inmediato |
| 5 | Indirect Cost (10%) | $6,800 | Admin overhead |

**Tip:** Use the **5‑line budget template** (core, direct, indirect, capital, other) to keep the budget easily readable.

### 8.3 Common Pitfalls  

- **Under‑budgeting:** Leave a buffer of 5–10 %.  
- **Over‑budgeting:** Avoid “splurging” on non‑essential items.  
- **Misaligned line items:** Every cost must link to a program activity.  
- **Omitting indirect costs:** Many funders require a percentage of direct costs.

## 11. Continuous Improvement  

### 11.1 Post‑Grant Evaluation  

1. **Collect feedback** from staff, participants, and the donor.  
2. **Measure outcomes** against targets.  
3. **Document lessons** using a “Grant Review” form.  

### 11.2 Lessons‑Learned Template

|----------|--------|-------------|
| What worked well? | Weekly group sessions increased engagement | Scale up to 6 months |

### 11.3 Updating Your Funder Database  

- **Add new contacts** after every interaction.  
- **Adjust fit scores** after each grant cycle.  
- **Schedule reminders** for future deadlines.
