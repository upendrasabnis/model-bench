## 1. The Grant‑Writing Landscape  

### Why Grants Matter

- **Capital for impact**: Grants provide non‑recurring funding that supports program expansion, infrastructure, or emergency response.
- **Non‑competitive leverage**: A well‑written grant can unlock matching funds, in‑kind donations, or partnerships.
- **Mission‑driven**: Grants enable you to focus on outcomes rather than “making ends meet.”

### The Grant Cycle

| Phase | Typical Duration | Key Activities |
|-------|------------------|----------------|
| **Research** | 4–6 weeks | Identify funders; assess fit |
| **Proposal Development** | 6–8 weeks | Write narrative; build budget |
| **Submission** | 1 week | Final checks; send |
| **Award Decision** | 2–4 weeks | Review, negotiation |
| **Reporting** | Ongoing (quarterly/monthly) | Track metrics; report |

*(Note: Some funders have “rolling deadlines.” Keep an eye on those for quick wins.)*

## 3. Crafting a Compelling Needs Statement  

The needs statement is **the first paragraph** that hooks the reader and **the justification** for the funding. It must be concise, data‑driven, and emotionally resonant.

### The “Problem‑Solution” Framework

| Step | What to Include | Example |
|------|-----------------|---------|
| **1. Define the problem** | Use statistics, anecdotes | “In 2023, 15% of children in Northville aged 5‑10 scored below the 25th percentile on reading proficiency tests.” |
| **2. Explain the impact** | Social, economic, health | “Low literacy leads to lower graduation rates, limiting employment prospects and perpetuating poverty.” |
| **3. Show the gap** | Why current resources fall short | “Existing after‑school programs lack qualified reading tutors, with only 2 tutors per 1,000 students.” |
| **4. Present the solution** | Your program’s core activity | “The Bright Futures Tutoring Initiative will recruit and train 50 community volunteers to provide bi‑weekly tutoring.” |
| **5. Quantify the need** | Numbers, timelines | “We aim to help 250 students improve reading scores by 10 points within 12 months.” |

## 4. Writing the Project Narrative  

A strong narrative tells a **story** that connects the needs statement to the *how* your organization will deliver results.

### Structure of the Narrative

| Section | Core Question | Content Tips |
|---------|---------------|--------------|
| **Vision** | What does success look like? | Use aspirational language (“Imagine a Northville where every child can read fluently”). |
| **Goals** | Broad outcome you aim for. | Keep one to three. |
| **Objectives** | Specific, measurable targets. | Use the **SMART** criteria. |
| **Activities** | What you’ll do. | List in chronological order. |
| **Outcomes** | What changes will happen. | Short‑term and long‑term. |
| **Impact** | The larger effect on the community. | Connect to the mission. |
| **Sustainability** | How it will continue after funding. | Partnerships, revenue streams. |

### Sample Narrative Excerpt

> **Goal**: Increase reading proficiency among Northville primary students.  
> **Objective 1**: Train 50 volunteers in evidence‑based tutoring techniques by July 2024.  
> **Activity 1.1**: Host a 3‑day intensive training workshop.  
> **Outcome 1.1**: 80% of volunteers will score ≥90% on the tutoring competency assessment.  
> **Impact**: By 2025, Northville’s reading proficiency rate will rise from 85% to 92%.

## 5. Building a Logic Model  

A logic model is a visual map that links **inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impact**. It clarifies your program logic and demonstrates that you have thought through the cause‑effect chain.

```
Inputs (Resources)
↓
Activities (What We Do)
↓
Outputs (What We Deliver)
↓
Outcomes (What Changes)
↓
Impact (Long‑Term Effect)
```

### 5‑Column Template

| Inputs | Activities | Outputs | Outcomes | Impact |
|--------|------------|---------|----------|--------|
| • 50 volunteers | • 3‑day training workshop | • 50 trained volunteers | • 80% pass competency test | • 7% overall reading score increase |
| • $12,000 grant | • 4 tutoring sessions per week | • 250 students tutored | • 10% improvement in reading scores | • Reduced dropout rates |
| • 4 classrooms | • Monthly parent workshops | • 200 parents engaged | • Parents supportive of reading home practices | •pygame |

**Checklist for a-book logic model**

- [ ] All columns are **clear** and **itialize**.  
- [ ] Each output directly follows an activity.  
- [ ] Outcomes are **measurable** (e.g., % improvement).  
- [ ] Impact links to your mission and community metrics.  
- [ ] The model is **utility** for the funder.  

*(Many foundations provide a logic‑model template in their RFP; use it if available.)*

## 6. Budget Development  

A realistic, transparent budget builds trust. Funders want to see that you can manage money responsibly.

### 6‑1. Budget Types

| Type | When to Use | Key Features |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Line‑Item Budget** | Most grant proposals | Detailed, itemized costs |
| **Cost‑Effectiveness Budget** | Foundations seeking sách | Shows cost per unit of outcome |
| **Revenue‑Expense Projection** | Multi‑year awards | Demonstrates sustainability |

### 6‑2. Building a Line‑Item Budget  

| Category | Example Items | Typical % of Total |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Personnel** | Program Manager, Tutoring Coordinator | 30–40% |
| **Contractual** | Volunteer training materials | 5–10% |
| **Supplies** | Textbooks, workbooks | 5–8% |
| **Travel** | Field visits, parent workshops | 3–5% |
| **Indirect Costs** | Overhead (if allowed) | 10–20% |
| **Contingency** | Unexpected expenses | 5% |

### 6‑4. Budget Justification  

- **Narrate** why each line item is essential.  
- **Link** budget items to the logic model.  
- **Explain** any indirect cost policy (e.g., 20% overhead).  

**Sample Excerpt**

Without Dub Manager, we would be unable to maintain the rigorous training and evaluation schedule required to achieve the 80% competency pass rate.”
