Thank you for reaching out and for the detailed description of the budget‑indirect‑cost issue you are confronting in your FY 2027 Health Equity Innovation Grant application. I appreciate the complexity of coordinating a multi‑partner consortium and have prepared the following clarification to address each of the four questions you raised.

**1. Use of a single, unified indirect cost rate**  
The Office of Management and Budget (OMB) Uniform Guidance permits a consortium of non‑profit entities to apply a single indirect cost (IDC) rate to the combined modified total direct costs of the project, provided that a “lead applicant” is designated and that the rate selected is consistent with the negotiated rate of at least one member of the consortium. In practice, most funding agencies allow the consortium to adopt the **lead organization’s negotiated rate** (14 % in your case) as the unified rate, or to apply a **provisional rate** that reflects the weighted average of all partners while negotiations are still in progress. If you choose the average approach, you must document the calculation and obtain written approval from the awarding agency before submission. Until the smaller partners finalize their rates, a provisional 10 % rate can be used, but you should note in the budget narrative that the rate is provisional and that the final rate will be applied retroactively once negotiations are complete.

**2. Presentation of indirect costs in the budget narrative**  
To remain compliant, the budget narrative should:  
- Identify the chosen IDC rate (e.g., “14 % of Modified Total Direct Costs, as negotiated by Riverside Community Health Center”) and explicitly state that this rate will be applied to the consortium’s total modified direct costs.  
- Explain the basis for the rate, referencing the OMB Uniform Guidance §200.404(c) provision that permits a unified rate for consortia.  
- Clarify that partner audit cycles do not affect the allowability of the IDC, as the rate is applied to the same modified direct cost base for all members.  
- Include a brief justification that any variation in audit timing will not impact the calculation of indirect costs, which is based on the same fiscal year’s modified direct costs.

**3. Ceiling limits and special considerations for larger awards**  
When total direct costs exceed $500,000, the indirect cost rate is still subject to the negotiated rate but must not exceed the **maximum allowable rate** set by the awarding agency’s policy. For most federal grants, the ceiling is typically **15 %** of Modified Total Direct Costs unless a higher rate has been formally approved. Because your projected budget is approximately $1.2 million, you should verify whether the awarding agency imposes a specific ceiling for this program; many Health Equity Innovation Grants cap IDC at 15 % regardless of award size. If a ceiling exists, you must adjust the rate accordingly or request a waiver with supporting justification.

**4. Documentation requirements**  
The awarding agency generally requires that **any supporting documentation for the indirect cost rate be attached to the budget** or submitted as an appendix. A consolidated statement from the lead applicant that includes:  
- The negotiated rate agreement (e.g., the award letter or negotiation memo from Riverside’s cognizant agency),  
- A summary of the rates for each partner (including the provisional 10 % rate for those not yet negotiated), and  
- A calculation worksheet showing the applied IDC to the consortium’s modified direct costs,  

will usually satisfy the documentation requirement. However, the agency may request a copy of each partner’s individual rate agreement if there is any doubt about the uniformity of the calculation. To avoid delays, I recommend attaching a single “Indirect Cost Rate Summary” that lists all partner rates and includes a footnote indicating that the consortium will adopt the lead applicant’s 14 % rate for the purpose of this submission, with a note that final rates will be reconciled post‑award.
