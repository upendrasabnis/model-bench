## 1. Introduction  

Grant writing is both an art and a science. It requires a deep understanding of a funder’s mission, the community need you aim to address, and the ability to translate vision into a compelling, fundable proposal. Successful nonprofit grant writers blend thorough research, strategic storytelling, and meticulous attention to detail.  

- **Why this guide matters:**  
  - Many nonprofits lose funding because their proposals fail to meet a funder’s specific criteria.  
  - A well‑crafted grant can unlock resources that sustain programs, expand reach, and strengthen organizational capacity.  
  - Best practices evolve; staying current with emerging trends (e.g., equity‑focused funding, digital reporting) keeps your organization competitive.  

- **What you’ll learn:**  
  - How to systematically research funders and identify the right matches.  
  - How to write a needs statement that resonates with funders’ priorities.  
  - Step‑by‑step guidance on budgets, logic models, evaluation plans, and staffing.  
  - Practical checklists and concrete examples you can adapt to any grant cycle.

## 2. Understanding the Grant Landscape  

Before you put pen to paper, you must understand the ecosystem of potential funders, the types of grants they offer, and the timing of their funding windows.  

### 2.1 Researching Funders  

| **Step** | **Action** | **Tools & Resources** | **Key Questions** |
|----------|------------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| 1️⃣ Identify Potential Funders | Use the Foundation Center ( Candid ) database, grant‑making public databases, and sector‑specific directories. | Candid Philanthropy, Foundation Directory Online, GrantStation | Which foundations have missions aligned with yours? |
| 2️⃣ Review Funding Priorities | Read annual reports, request for proposals (RFPs), and online guidelines. | Funder websites, PDF archives, newsletters | What specific issues, geographies, or populations do they fund? |
| 3️⃣ Note Eligibility & Requirements | Capture minimum budget, organizational type, fiscal year, matching requirements. | Funder FAQs, eligibility matrices | Do you meet the “in‑state,” “non‑profit,” “budget > $X” criteria? |
| 4️⃣ Track Deadlines & Cycles | Use a spreadsheet or grant‑management software (e.g., Submittable, Grantium). | Google Calendar, Asana, Trello | When is the application due? How often do they accept proposals? |
| 5️⃣ Understand Reporting & Compliance | Review past report templates, audit requirements, and any sub‑grant rules. | Funder’s reporting portal, compliance manuals | What metrics must you report? Are there site visits? |

**Concrete Example:**  
You run a youth mentorship nonprofit serving low‑income teens in Chicago. You find the *Robert Wood Johnson Foundation* has a “Youth Empowerment” priority with a $2 M grant max, requiring a 20% cash match and a site visit. You note that the foundation also expects quarterly progress reports and a logic model. You add this funder to your spreadsheet with columns for “Match %,” “Reporting Frequency,” and “Contact Person.”  

### 2.2 Types of Grants & Funding Cycles  

| **Grant Type** | **Typical Features** | **Best For** |
|----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **Program‑Specific Grants** | Tied to a single initiative; detailed budgets required. | New or expanding programs. |
| **Operating Grants** | Support core overhead; fewer program restrictions. | Organizations needing stable funding. |
| **Capacity‑Building Grants** | Funds staff training, technology, board development. | Organizations seeking to strengthen infrastructure. |
| **Endowment / Challenge Grants** | Require a matching contribution; often long‑term. | Large capital campaigns. |
| **Multi‑Year Grants** | Funding over 2‑5 years; require annual reports. | Sustainable, proven programs. |
| **Competitive Fellowships / Grants** | Small awards, high competition, often project‑based. | Pilot projects or research. |

**Funding Cycles:**  
- **Annual Cycles:** Most foundations release one RFP per year (e.g., *Kellogg Foundation*).  
- **Rolling Admissions:** Some funders accept applications continuously (e.g., *Google.org* grants).  
- **Seasonal Peaks:** Summer/fall are common for school‑year programs; spring for fiscal‑year planning.  

**Practical Checklist – Fund Research:**  

- [ ] Create a master list of at least 15 potential funders.  
- [ ] For each funder, document mission, priority areas, eligibility, and deadline.  
- [ ] Assign a “funder champion” (team member) responsible for tracking updates.  
- [ ] Set up calendar alerts for each deadline and reporting due date.  
- [ ] Keep a “Funder Profile” template (PDF) with contact info, website, and key contacts.

## 3. Crafting the Foundation of Your Proposal  

A strong proposal starts with a solid foundation: clear mission alignment, a compelling needs statement, and well‑defined goals and objectives.  

### 3.1 Mission, Vision & Alignment  

1. **Mirror the Funder’s Language** – Use the same terminology the funder uses to describe their priority.  
2. **Show Strategic Alignment** – Explain *how* your program advances the funder’s strategic plan (e.g., “Our after‑school tutoring aligns with the foundation’s goal of improving STEM literacy by 2025”).  
3. **Include a Vision Statement** – Briefly articulate the long‑term change you envision for the community.  

**Example:**  

> *Mission:* “Empower underserved youth through high‑quality STEM education.”  
> *Alignment:* “Our after‑school robotics program directly supports the *National Science Foundation’s* objective to increase K‑12 student engagement in engineering by 30% in under‑represented communities.”  

### 3.2 Needs Statement & Assessment  

The needs statement is the persuasive heart of your proposal. It must be data‑driven, specific, and urgent.  

**Components:**  

| **Component** | **What to Include** | **Example** |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Problem Definition** | Clear description of the issue, scope, and impact. | “In Chicago’s South Side, 68% of high‑school students score below grade level in math, limiting college and career options.” |
| **Data & Sources** | Statistics, research, community surveys, stakeholder interviews. | “Data from the Illinois State Board of Education (2023) and a local parent focus group of 12 families.” |
| **Geographic/Temporal Focus** | Where and when the problem exists. | “Focusing on ages 13‑18 in the Near West Side neighborhoods.” |
| **Why It Matters Now** | Urgency, trends, or impending consequences. | “Without intervention, 45% of these students drop out before graduation, perpetuating cycles of poverty.” |
| **Gap in Services** | What is currently missing in the community. | “Only two after‑school STEM clubs exist, serving <200 students annually.” |

**Concrete Example (Needs Statement):**  

> “In the city’s underserved neighborhoods, 72% of middle‑school students lack access to hands‑on robotics education, a gap that contributes to a 30% lower college enrollment rate in STEM fields. Existing community programs can accommodate only 150 students per year, leaving an estimated 500 qualified youth without opportunities. This unmet need threatens both individual career prospects and the region’s future workforce pipeline.”  

**Checklist – Needs Statement:**  

- [ ] Use at least three credible data sources.  
- [ ] Quantify the problem (percentages, numbers).  
- [ ] Tie the problem to the funder’s priority (e.g., equity, education).  
- [ ] Highlight any existing service gaps.  
- [ ] Keep the statement to 2–3 paragraphs for readability.  

### 3.3 Goals, Objectives & Outcomes  

Goals are broad, long‑term aspirations; objectives are SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time‑bound) statements that lead to outcomes.  

**Template Example:**  

> **Goal:** Increase STEM literacy among underrepresented high‑school students in Chicago by 40% over five years.  
> **Objective 1:** Provide after‑school robotics workshops to 300 students annually by Fall 2025.  
> **Objective 2:** Achieve a 75% improvement in participants’ standardized math scores within one academic year.  
> **Outcome Indicator:** Pre‑ and post‑program assessment scores (baseline vs. post‑intervention).  

**Best Practice Tips:**  

- **Link each objective directly to the need.**  
- **Use outcome language** (“increase,” “reduce,” “improve”) rather than activity language (“offer,” “host”).  
- **Include baseline data** and target metrics.

## 4. Developing the Program Narrative  

The program narrative tells the story of *how* you will achieve your objectives. It should be clear, logical, and compelling.  

### 4.1 Description of Need (Revisited)  

- **Re‑state the need** in the context of the program you are proposing.  
- **Explain why this program is the solution** to that need.  

### 4.2 Program Approach & Strategy  

**Key Elements:**  

| **Element** | **Content** | **Tip** |
|-------------|------------|--------|
| **Program Model** | Overview of the intervention (e.g., curriculum, staffing, delivery method). | Use bullet points or a simple flowchart. |
| **Innovative Components** | What makes your approach unique (technology, partnership, cultural relevance). | Emphasize sustainability and scalability. |
| **Evidence‑Based Practices** | Reference proven methodologies or research supporting your model. | Cite peer‑reviewed studies or respected frameworks (e.g., Positive Youth Development). |
| **Target Population** | Demographics, geographic location, inclusion criteria. | Show you understand the community’s nuances. |
| **Exclusion Criteria** | Clarify who will not be served (helps manage expectations). | Helpful for funders assessing equity impact. |

**Example:**  

> “Our **STEM Explorers** program will deliver a 12‑week after‑school robotics curriculum, combining hands‑on building, coding, and mentorship. The curriculum is adapted from the **FIRST LEGO League** framework, proven to increase student interest in engineering by 45% (Source: *Journal of STEM Education*, 2022). We will partner with local high‑school volunteer mentors, ensuring cultural relevance and role‑model exposure.”  

### 4.3 Implementation Timeline  

A timeline visualizes when each activity will occur. Use a simple Gantt‑style table or chronological bullet list.  

| **Month** | **Activity** | **Owner** |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| **Month 1** | Needs assessment & stakeholder interviews | Program Manager |
| **Month 2** | Curriculum design & volunteer recruitment | Curriculum Lead |
| **Month 3** | Pilot workshop (20 students) | Field Staff |
| **Month 4‑9** | Full program rollout (3 cohorts) | Program Team |
| **Month 10** | Data collection & analysis | Evaluation Specialist |
| **Month 11** | Reporting & program refinement | All Staff |
| **Month 12** | Grant close‑out & sustainability planning | Executive Director |

**Checklist – Program Narrative:**  

- [ ] Include a clear description of the program model.  
- [ ] Highlight any partnerships or collaborations.  
- [ ] Provide evidence that the approach is research‑backed.  
- [ ] Define target and exclusion criteria.  
- [ ] Add a visual timeline (Gantt, calendar, or bullet list).  
- [ ] Ensure language is jargon‑free and accessible.

## 5. Building a Realistic Budget  

A budget is more than a spreadsheet; it’s a story of resource allocation that demonstrates fiscal responsibility and feasibility.  

### 5.1 Direct & Indirect Costs  

| **Cost Category** | **Typical Items** | **Documentation Tips** |
|-------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| **Personnel** | Salaries, benefits, stipends, fringe benefits | Provide salary justification matrix; include market rate comparisons. |
| **Materials & Supplies** | LEGO kits, software licenses, lab consumables | Itemize quantity and unit cost; reference vendor quotes. |
| **Travel** | Field trips, conference attendance, participant transportation | Include purpose, dates, and per‑diem rates. |
| **Rent & Utilities** | Space for after‑school sessions, electricity, internet | Provide lease agreement excerpts; note any shared‑resource agreements. |
| **Equipment** | Laptops, 3‑D printers, robotics kits | Include depreciation schedule if applicable. |
| **Indirect (Overhead)** | Administrative salaries, rent for office space, utilities | Use a reasonable indirect cost rate (e.g., 10‑15% of direct costs) unless negotiated. |

**Example Budget Table (Simplified):**  

| **Category** | **Year 1** | **Year 2** | **Year 3** |
|--------------|------------|------------|------------|
| Personnel (Program Director) | $60,000 | $60,000 | $60,000 |
| Stipends (10 mentors @ $500) | $5,000 | $5,000 | $5,000 |
| Materials (LEGO kits, sensors) | $12,000 | $8,000 | $8,000 |
| Rent (classroom) | $6,000 | $6,000 | $6,000 |
| Evaluation (external consultant) | $8,000 | $4,000 | $4,000 |
| **Total Direct Costs** | **$91,000** | **$83,000** | **$83,000** |
| **Indirect (10%)** | $9,100 | $8,300 | $8,300 |
| **Grand Total** | **$100,100** | **$91,300** | **$91,300** |

### 5.2 Budget Narrative  

The budget narrative explains each line item, justifying costs and demonstrating how they align with program activities.  

**Sample Narrative (for Personnel):**  

> “The Program Director’s salary of $60,000 per year reflects the market rate for similar roles in Chicago’s nonprofit sector (based on a 2023 survey by the Chicago Nonprofit Association). This position will oversee program design, mentor recruitment, and stakeholder communication, ensuring consistent delivery across all cohorts.”  

**Best Practices:**  

- **Link each budget line to a specific activity or outcome.**  
- **Provide supporting documentation** (e.g., quotes, salary scales) in an attachment.  
- **Avoid “blanket” categories**; be specific (e.g., “3‑D printer – $2,500” vs. “Equipment – $2,500”).  

### 5.3 Cost Allocation & Justification  

- **Indirect Cost Rate:** If the organization has an established indirect cost rate (e.g., 15% approved by the IRS), use that. Otherwise, request a negotiated rate.  
- **Cost Sharing/Match:** Clearly state any required cash or in‑kind match and how it will be documented.  

**Example Justification for a 3‑D Printer:**  

> “The 3‑D printer (Model XYZ, $2,500) will enable students to prototype robotics components, directly supporting the curriculum’s hands‑on learning objective. The printer will be used across three cohorts annually, amortized over three years, resulting in an annual cost of $833.”  

**Checklist – Budget Section:**  

- [ ] Provide a clear table with line items, quantities, unit costs, and totals.  
- [ ] Include a narrative for each major line item.  
- [ ] Attach supporting documents (quotes, salary benchmarks).  
- [ ] Verify indirect cost rate compliance.  
- [ ] Show how the budget aligns with program timeline and goals.

## 6. Logic Model Development  

A logic model is a visual roadmap that connects resources, activities, outputs, outcomes, and impact. It helps funders see the theory of change and enables you to evaluate success.  

### 6.1 What Is a Logic Model?  

A **Logic Model** typically includes five components:  

1. **Inputs** – Resources (funding, staff, materials).  
2. **Activities** – What you will do (workshops, trainings).  
3. **Outputs** – Direct, immediate products (number of students served, kits built).  
4. **Outcomes** – Short‑ and long‑term changes (improved test scores, increased college interest).  
5. **Impact** – Broader community or systemic change.  

### 6.2 Core Components  

| **Component** | **Key Questions** | **Example (STEM Explorers)** |
|---------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| **Inputs** | What resources will you bring in? | $100,100 funding, 2 FTE program staff, 10 volunteer mentors, LEGO/Arduino kits. |
| **Activities** | What will you do with those resources? | Design curriculum, recruit mentors, conduct 12 weekly after‑school sessions, facilitate robotics builds. |
| **Outputs** | What immediate products result? | 300 students served annually, 150 robotics kits assembled, 12 mentor training sessions. |
| **Outcomes** | What changes will happen? | • **Knowledge:** 75% increase in participants’ STEM vocabulary scores.<br>• **Attitudes:** 60% report higher confidence in pursuing STEM careers.<br>• **Behavior:** 30% enroll in advanced STEM courses. |
| **Impact** | What broader effect? | Increased pipeline of underrepresented students into local tech internships; measurable contribution to Chicago’s 2030 STEM workforce goals. |

### 6.3 Example Logic Model (Step‑by‑Step)  

1. **Start with Inputs** – List everything you need to deliver the program.  
2. **Map Activities** – Choose the sequence that transforms inputs into outputs.  
3. **Define Outputs** – Quantify what you will produce (e.g., “Number of workshops held”).  
4. **Identify Outcomes** – Use SMART indicators (see Section 7).  
5. **Consider External Factors** – Note any assumptions or barriers (e.g., school calendar, partner availability).  

**Visual Representation (text‑based):**  

```
[Inputs] --> [Activities] --> [Outputs] --> [Outcomes] --> [Impact]

Inputs:
- $100,100 grant
- 2 Program Staff
- 10 Volunteer Mentors
- Materials (kits, software)

Activities:
- Curriculum design (Month 1)
- Mentor training (Month 2)
- 12 weekly after‑school sessions (Months 3‑14)
- Evaluation data collection (Months 12‑14)

Outputs:
- 300 students served
- 150 robotics kits built
- 12 mentor training sessions completed
- 1 program handbook distributed

Outcomes:
- Knowledge: +75% STEM vocab scores
- Attitude: +60% confidence in STEM careers
- Behavior: +30% enrollment in advanced STEM courses

Impact:
- Strengthened local STEM pipeline
- Contribution to citywide workforce diversity goals
```  

**Checklist – Logic Model:**  

- [ ] List all inputs (financial, human, material).  
- [ ] Sequence activities logically.  
- [ ] Quantify outputs with numbers.  
- [ ] Define measurable outcomes (use SMART criteria).  
- [ ] Connect outcomes directly to the original problem.  
- [ ] Include assumptions and potential barriers.

## 7. Evaluation & Measurement  

A robust evaluation plan demonstrates accountability and informs program improvement. It should be integrated into the logic model and align with funder requirements.  

### 7.1 Indicators & Metrics  

| **Outcome** | **Indicator** | **Data Source** | **Target** | **Timeframe** |
|------------|---------------|----------------|------------|---------------|
| Knowledge Gain | Pre/post STEM vocabulary scores | Standardized assessment | +75% improvement | End of program (Month 14) |
| Attitude Change | Self‑efficacy survey (Likert scale) | Participant survey | Average score ≥ 4.0/5 | Mid‑program (Month 7) and End |
| Behavioral Intention | Enrollment in advanced STEM courses | School transcripts | +30% increase | 6 months post‑program |
| Program Reach | Number of unique students served | Registration logs | 300 students | Year 1 |
| Mentor Impact | Mentee academic performance | Mentor reports | +10% grade point increase | End of Year 2 |

**Tips for Indicator Selection:**  

- **Choose indicators that are feasible to collect** (e.g., use existing school data rather than creating new assessments).  
- **Ensure indicators are aligned with funder priorities** (e.g., equity metrics if funder focuses on underserved communities).  
- **Use both quantitative and qualitative data** (surveys, focus groups, observations).  

### 7.2 Data Collection Methods  

| **Method** | **What It Captures** | **Frequency** | **Tools** |
|------------|----------------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Surveys** | Attitudes, satisfaction, knowledge | Pre, mid, post | Google Forms, SurveyMonkey |
| **Tests/Assessments** | Knowledge gain | Pre & post | Standardized STEM test, custom quiz |
| **Focus Groups** | Deep insights, barriers, facilitators | End of program | Guide, audio recorder |
| **Observations** | Implementation fidelity, engagement | Random sessions | Observation checklist |
| **Document Review** | Participation rates, grades | Ongoing | School transcripts, attendance sheets |
| **Interviews** | Stakeholder perspectives | Year‑end | Interview guide, recorder |

**Example Data Collection Timeline:**  

| **Month** | **Activity** |
|-----------|--------------|
| 0 (Pre‑Program) | Administer pre‑survey and baseline knowledge test. |
| 3 | Mid‑program focus group with 8 participants. |
| 7 | Mid‑program satisfaction survey. |
| 12 | Post‑program survey, knowledge test, and behavioral intention survey. |
| 14 | End‑of‑program focus group & final data analysis. |
| Ongoing | Update participant tracking spreadsheet. |

### 7.3 Reporting Mechanisms  

- **Quarterly Progress Reports** – Brief narrative + indicator updates.  
- **Annual Report** – Comprehensive assessment, financial summary, lessons learned.  
- **Evaluation Report** – Full methodological rigor, findings, recommendations.  
- **Funder-Specific Dashboards** – Some funders provide online portals for real‑time data (e.g., *Bloomberg Philanthropies*).  

**Best Practices:**  

- **Start data collection early** to avoid last‑minute scrambling.  
- **Use consistent definitions** across all reports (e.g., “students served” = unique individuals).  
- **Include visual data** (charts, graphs) to make reports digestible.  
- **Provide context** for any setbacks (e.g., pandemic, staffing changes).  

**Checklist – Evaluation Plan:**  

- [ ] Identify all required reports from each funder.  
- [ ] Choose data sources that are reliable and valid.  
- [ ] Develop data collection tools (surveys, checklists) and pilot test.  
- [ ] Create a data management plan (spreadsheet, database).  
- [ ] Set up regular data cleaning and analysis schedule.  
- [ ] Draft reporting templates in advance.

## 8. Management, Staffing & Organizational Capacity  

Even the best program will falter without the right people, systems, and oversight.  

### 8.1 Key Personnel Profiles  

| **Role** | **Primary Responsibilities** | **Qualifications** | **Time Commitment** |
|----------|------------------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Executive Director** | Strategic oversight, fiduciary responsibility, donor relations | 5+ years nonprofit leadership | 20% of time (budget approval) |
| **Program Director** | Day‑to‑day program implementation, staff supervision | Master’s in Education/Nonprofit Management | 100% FTE |
| **Curriculum Specialist** | Design and refine educational content | STEM background, curriculum design certification | 30% FTE (part‑time) |
| **Mentor Coordinator** | Recruit, train, and manage volunteer mentors | Experience in youth development | 20% FTE |
| **Evaluation Specialist** | Data collection, analysis, reporting | M.S. in Research Methods | 25% FTE |
| **Administrative Assistant** | Grant tracking, invoicing, office management | Associate’s degree in Business | 50% FTE |

**Example Staffing Narrative:**  

> “Our organization has a proven track record of delivering high‑impact STEM programs. The Program Director, Jane Doe, holds a Master’s in Education and has previously managed after‑school initiatives serving over 500 students. The Evaluation Specialist, Dr. Alan Liu, brings expertise in quasi‑experimental design, ensuring rigorous measurement of program outcomes.”  

### 8.2 Roles, Responsibilities & Overlap  

- **Clear reporting lines** reduce confusion and improve accountability.  
- **RACI matrix** (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) can be used for grant‑specific tasks.  

**Sample RACI for Grant Implementation:**  

| **Task** | **RACI** |
|----------|----------|
| Drafting the proposal | R: Program Director; A: Executive Director; C: Finance; I: Board |
| Budget preparation | R: Finance Manager; A: Program Director; C: External Auditor; I: Board |
| Sub‑grant recruitment | R: Mentor Coordinator; A: Program Director; C: Executive Director; I: Board |
| Data collection | R: Evaluation Specialist; A: Program Director; C: Teachers; I: Funders |

### 8.3 Volunteer & Partner Contributions  

- **Document volunteer hours** as in‑kind contributions (valued at market rate).  
- **Partner agreements** should outline scope, responsibilities, and deliverables.  

**Example Volunteer Contribution:**  

> “Ten retired engineers will volunteer an average of 4 hours per week for 12 weeks, providing hands‑on mentorship. Their total contribution is valued at $4,800 (based on average hourly wage of $15), counted as in‑kind match toward the funder’s 20% cash‑match requirement.”  

**Checklist – Staffing & Capacity:**  

- [ ] Identify all roles needed for successful implementation.  
- [ ] Verify qualifications and create job descriptions.  
- [ ] Develop a staffing plan with FTE percentages.  
- [ ] Document volunteer/in‑kind contributions and valuations.  
- [ ] Create a RACI matrix for key grant tasks.  
- [ ] Ensure background checks or liability coverage where required.

## 9. Sustainability & Leverage Strategies  

A grant should not be a one‑time lifeline; it should catalyze long‑term viability.  

### 9.1 Funding Diversification  

| **Strategy** | **Implementation Steps** | **Timeline** |
|--------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| **Identify Additional Funders** | Map other foundations, corporate CSR, government grants. | Ongoing; add 3–5 new prospects per quarter. |
| **Develop a “Pipeline” of Small Grants** | Write micro‑grants (e.g., $5k–$10k) for specific activities. | Quarterly cycles. |
| **Create a Recurring Donor Program** | Launch monthly giving campaign, stewardship newsletters. | 6‑12 months to launch. |
| **Host Fundraising Events** | Annual gala, auction, crowdfunding campaign. | Annually or bi‑annually. |
| **Strategic Alliances** | Form consortia with other nonprofits to share resources. | 3‑6 months to negotiate. |

### 9.2 Community & In‑Kind Support  

- **In‑Kind Donations:** Supplies, space, professional services.  
- **Community Partnerships:** Local schools, libraries, businesses.  

**Example Leverage Plan:**  

> “We have secured a 10% discount on LEGO education kits from a local distributor, saving $12,000. Additionally, a nearby university will provide free statistical analysis services for evaluation, valued at $8,000.”  

### 9.3 Long‑Term Viability Plan  

- **Financial Modeling:** Project cash flow for 3–5 years, showing how the grant fits into the budget.  
- **Program Replication:** Document successful components for scaling to new sites.  
- **Capacity Building:** Invest in staff training, technology upgrades, and board development.  

**Checklist – Sustainability:**  

- [ ] Draft a 3‑year financial projection.  
- [ ] Identify at least three additional funding sources (foundation, corporate, government).  
- [ ] Secure at least one in‑kind contribution valued >$5,000.  
- [ ] Outline a plan for program replication or expansion.  
- [ ] Create a board development agenda (training, fundraising).

<a name="post-award-management"></a>  

## 10. Post‑Award Management  

Winning a grant is only the beginning. Effective post‑award management ensures compliance, demonstrates impact, and positions you for future funding.  

### 10.1 Grant Reporting Requirements  

| **Report Type** | **Frequency** | **Key Elements** | **Tips** |
|-----------------|---------------|------------------|----------|
| **Progress Narrative** | Quarterly | Program updates, challenges, outcomes to date | Use template; attach photos or participant quotes. |

### 10.2 Financial Reporting  

- **Segregation of Funds:** Keep grant money in a separate bank account or ledger.  
- **Expense Documentation:** Retain receipts, invoices, and approval forms.  
- **Budget Modifications:** If you need to reallocate funds, obtain written approval from the funder.  

**Example Budget Modification Request:**  

> “Due to unforeseen shipping delays, the cost of robotics kits has increased by $2,000. We propose reallocating $2,000 from the ‘Materials’ line to the ‘Equipment’ line, preserving total expenditures while ensuring program quality.”  

### 10.3 Narrative & Progress Reports  

**Structure (Example):**  

1. **Program Highlights (Last Quarter)** – Specific activities, number of participants, key quotes.  
2. **Challenges & Solutions** – Explain any obstacles and how they were addressed.  
3. **Outcomes & Indicators** – Update on target metrics (e.g., “STEM vocab scores improved by 45%, nearing the 75% target”).  
4. **Next Steps** – What’s planned for the next reporting period.  

**Best Practices:**  

- **Be concise** (1–2 pages per report).  
- **Use bullet points** for readability.  
- **Include visual evidence** (photos, charts).  
- **Proofread** for grammar, spelling, and funder branding.

- **Compliance Checks:** Verify that all expenditures align with allowable costs (e.g., travel per policy).
