## 1. What Is Acquittal Reporting?

### 1.1 Definition

Acquittal reporting is the formal submission of financial and narrative evidence that a funded project has been executed in accordance with the terms of the grant agreement. It is **not** a "clean‑up" of the project; it is a **statement of compliance** that the grantee has spent money according to theobservable, measurable, and verifiable guidelines set by the funder.

Think of it as a **financial audit** that accompanies the *technical* or *research* report. While the technical report showcases results, the acquittal report demonstrates that the money used to produce those results was spent properly.

### 1.2 Legal Background

- **Federal Grants**: Governed by the **Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR)**, the **Uniform Administrative Requirements, Cost Principles, and Audit Requirements for Federal Awards (Uniform Guidance)**, and the **Office of Management and Budget (OMB) Circulars** (e.g., OMB A-133, A-1330).
- **State Grants**: State agencies usually adopt the uniform guidance or a state‑specific version (e.g., California’s Department of Education uses the *California Administrative Code*).
- **Philanthropic Foundations**: Foundations set their own compliance guidelines, but many align with the Uniform Guidance for consistency.

## 4. Record‑Keeping Foundations

### 4.1 Budget & Expense Tracking

- Maintain a **centralized budget spreadsheet** that mirrors the grant agreement’s budget line items.
- Use **accounting software** (e.g., QuickBooks, Xero, or a specialized grant‑management system like **Blackbaud Grantmaking**).
- **Reconcile** quarterly: the *budgeted* vs. *actual* spending.

### 4.2 Time‑Tracking & Labor

- Use a **time‑tracking system** (e.g., **Toggl**, **Harvest**, or a custom *Labor Tracking Module*).
- Record **hours** for each employee and contractor on each grant.
- Ensure labor is classified correctly: *direct*, *indirect*, *unrestricted*.

**Real‑World Example**: The University of Texas at Austin had a federal **NSF** grant that required a **Labor Allocation Report**. They used a custom spreadsheet that mapped each researcher’s hours to the grant’s cost categories. The system flagged any mis‑allocation >5% early, preventing a costly audit.

### 4.3 Travel & Subsistence

- Maintain a **Travel Log** with:
  - Purpose, destination, dates.
  - Expense categories (airfare, lodging, per diem, mileage).
  - Receipts (digital or scanned).
- Travel must comply with the funder’s **Travel Policy** (e.g., maximum per diem, overnight stays_COMPLEX).

**Example**: In 2022, a nonprofit that received an EPA grant for a **Clean Air** project had to submit a *Travel Report* that included a per diem of $45 for each day of travel. They used a travel management software that automatically generated the report.

### 4.4 Procurement & Vendor Management

- Maintain a **Vendor Master File** that includes:
  - Vendor contact details.
  - Contract terms (price, deliverablesacco).
  - Approved vendor status (approved by the finance office).
- Ensure **Purchase Orders** (POs) are linked to the grant and that invoices are matched to POs before payment.

**Case Study**: The state of Colorado’s public health department needed to submit a **P5** procurement audit for a grant. They had to provide a *vendor matrix* showing compliance with the **Colorado Public Procurement Act**. Their vendor file met the audit, and no penalties were applied.

### 4.5 Data & Study Materials

- For research grants, keep a record of:
  - Data sets, protocols, IRB approvals.
  - Data ownership and licensing agreements.
- Many funders require a **Data Management Plan (DMP)** and a **Data Sharing Statement**.

**Examplepilot**: The NSF’s *REU* (Research Experiences for Undergraduates) grant required a **Data Sharing Statement** that the university had to include in the final report. The university’s data repository (e.g., **Figshare**) was integrated, and all datasets were archived and publicly accessible.
