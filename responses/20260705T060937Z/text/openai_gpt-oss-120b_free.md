## 1. Understanding the CDBG Program  

### 1.1. History & Purpose  

| Year | Milestone | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| 1974 | **Housing and Community Development Act** created CDBG | First federal block‑grant program aimed at “benefiting low‑ and moderate‑income persons, preventing or eliminating slums, and encouraging economic development.” |
| 1994 | HUD Reauthorization Act expands eligible activities | Allows funding for public facilities, economic development, and disaster recovery. |
| 2010‑2020 | HUD “CDBG 2020” cycle emphasizes “community‑driven planning” | Places greater weight on community participation, data‑driven needs assessments, and measurable outcomes. |
| 2024 | HUD releases **CDBG‑2025** guidance (still pending final rule) | Anticipates increased emphasis on climate‑resilient infrastructure and broadband access. |

**Core Goals** (as defined by HUD):  

1. **Benefiting Low‑ and Moderate‑Income (LMI) Persons** – at least 70 % of CDBG funds must be directed to LMI beneficiaries.  
2. **Preventing or Eliminating Slums/Blighted Areas** – revitalization of deteriorated neighborhoods.  
3. **Promoting Economic Development** – jobs, business growth, and diversification.  

### 1.2. Funding Structure & Allocation  

- **Annual Allocation Formula** – based on population, poverty rate, housing stock, and fiscal capacity.  
- **State Allocation** – HUD distributes to states; states allocate to eligible municipalities (cities, towns, counties).  
- **Typical Grant Size for a Small Midwestern Town (pop. < 15,000)** – $250 K – $2.5 M per year, depending on need and program cycle.  

### 1.3. Eligibility Criteria for Municipalities  

| Requirement | Detail | “Yes/No” Decision Point |
|-------------|--------|------------------------|
| **Incorporated Entity** | Must be an incorporated city, town, or county. | ✔ |
| **Population Threshold** | No formal cap, but very small towns (< 1,000) may be deemed ineligible unless designated as “rural.” | ✔/✘ |
| **Fiscal Capacity** | Must demonstrate ability to manage grant funds (e.g., audited financial statements, internal controls). | ✔ |
| **Prior CDBG Performance** | No default on previous HUD grants; must have submitted required annual reports. | ✔ |
| **Geographic Eligibility** | Must be located within a HUD‑designated “State” (all 50 states, DC, PR). | ✔ |
| **LMI Focus** | Must have a documented LMI population ≥ 30 % of total residents. | ✔/✘ |

## 2. Step‑by‑Step Guide to a Competitive CDBG Application  

> **Tip:** Keep a master spreadsheet of deadlines, responsible staff, and status (see Gantt‑chart template in Section 5.2).  

### 2.1. Pre‑Application Planning  

1. **Confirm Funding Window** – HUD releases the “Notice of Funding Availability (NOFA)” each fiscal year (usually May‑June).  
2. **Identify a “Lead Agency”** – typically the Planning & Development Department; assign a **CDBG Project Manager (PM)**.  
3. **Set a Realistic Timeline** – 6‑month window from NOFA release to submission is typical for small towns.  

### 2.2. Needs Assessment & Data Collection  

| Data Source | Metric | Why It Matters | How to Gather |
|-------------|--------|----------------|---------------|
| U.S. Census (ACS) | % LMI households, median income | Establishes eligibility & target population | Download from data.census.gov |
| HUD‑I (Housing Data) | Housing vacancy, code violations | Identifies blighted areas | Request from local HUD‑I office |
| Local Economic Development Corp. | Unemployment, industry mix | Shows need for diversification | Survey businesses, review BLS data |
| GIS Mapping | Walkability, broadband gaps | Visual tool for community input | Use ArcGIS or free QGIS |
| Community Survey | Resident priorities (housing, jobs, safety) | Demonstrates community‑driven planning | Online (Qualtrics) + paper outreach |

**Deliverable:** A **Comprehensive Needs Assessment Report (CNAR)** – 10‑15 pages, with charts, maps, and a clear problem statement.

### 2.3. Project Concept Development  

| Concept Element | Question to Answer | Example (Town B) |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Goal** | What overarching outcome do we want? | “Reduce vacant housing stock by 30 % within 5 years.” |
| **Objectives** | Specific, measurable, time‑bound (SMART). | “Rehab 15 abandoned homes by 2026.” |
| **Activities** | Concrete steps to achieve objectives. | “Provide 0‑% interest loans to developers; offer façade‑improvement grants.” |
| **Outputs** | Tangible products. | “15 rehabilitated units, 10 façade grants awarded.” |
| **Outcomes** | Short‑ and long‑term impacts. | “10 % increase in owner‑occupied housing; 5 new jobs created.” |

### 2.4. Building the Application Team  

| Role | Primary Responsibilities | Typical Staff |
|------|--------------------------|---------------|
| **Project Manager** | Timeline, budget, compliance | Planning Director |
| **Economic Development Analyst** | CBA, market study | ED Coordinator |
| **Grant Writer** | Narrative, narrative editing | External consultant (part‑time) |
| **Finance Officer** | Matching fund verification, cash‑flow | City Treasurer |
| **Community Liaison** | Stakeholder meetings, letters of support | Community Development Officer |
| **GIS Specialist** | Maps, spatial data | IT/Planning staff |

**Action:** Draft a **Team Charter** (one‑page) that outlines roles, decision‑making authority, and communication cadence (weekly check‑ins).  

### 2.5. Drafting Narrative Sections  

| Section | Key Content | Word‑Count Guidance |
|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Cover Letter** | Intro, grant amount requested, brief project tagline | 150‑200 |
| **Executive Summary** | Problem, solution, requested funds, LMI impact | 300‑400 |
| **Statement of Need** | Data‑driven justification; maps & charts | 800‑1,000 |
| **Project Description** | Goals, objectives, activities, timeline, staffing | 1,200‑1,500 |
| **Beneficiary Description** | LMI definition, how they will benefit | 300‑500 |
| **Project Management Plan** | Roles, procurement, reporting | 400‑600 |
| **Financial Plan** | Budget, matching, cost‑share, sustainability | 500‑700 |
| **Evaluation Plan** | Metrics, data sources, reporting schedule | 300‑400 |
| **Attachments** | Letters of support, certifications, maps | N/A |

**Writing Tips:**  

- Use **active voice** and **plain language** (HUD reviewers read hundreds of applications).  
- Cite **specific numbers** (“15 vacant lots, 12 % vacancy rate”).  
- Align every activity with at least one of the three core CDBG goals.  

### 2.6. Preparing Financial Packages  

1. **Draft Budget (see Section 5.1).**  
2. **Identify Matching Sources** – local cash, in‑kind contributions, other federal/state grants.  
3. **Develop a “Cash‑Flow Projection”** – show when funds will be drawn, disbursed, and reimbursed.  
4. **Attach Audited Financial Statements** (most recent FY).  

### 2.7. Review, Edit, and Submit  

| Review Stage | Who? | Focus |
|--------------|------|-------|
| **Internal Peer Review** | PM + Finance Officer | Completeness, budget math |
| **Legal/Compliance Check** | City Attorney | Certifications, de‑barred parties |
| **External Reviewer** | Regional HUD office (optional) | Alignment with HUD priorities |
| **Final Formatting** | Grant Writer | PDF conversion, page limits |
| **Submission** | PM (via HUD’s **eGrants** portal) | Confirm receipt, capture confirmation number |

## 3. Financing Mechanics  

### 3.1. Required Matching Funds  

| Funding Source | Typical Match Ratio* | Example Contribution (Town C) |
|----------------|----------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Local Cash** | 5 %–10 % of total project cost | $150,000 cash for $1.5 M project |
| **In‑Kind Services** | Valued at fair market | Engineering services ($75,000) |
| **State/Regional Grants** | Up to 30 % of total | State Revitalization Grant $300,000 |
| **Private Foundations** | Variable | Community Foundation $100,000 |
| **Tax Increment Financing (TIF)** | Up to 20 % | TIF bond revenue $200,000 |

\*HUD does **not** prescribe a fixed match rate for CDBG; the match must be “reasonable and documented.”  

**Documentation Checklist:**  

- Bank statements or cash‑flow statements for local cash.  
- In‑kind contribution letters with detailed scope and valuation.  
- Copies of other grant award letters.  

### 3.2. Cost‑Benefit Analysis (CBA) Framework  

| Step | Description | Tools |
|------|-------------|-------|
| **1. Identify Costs** | Direct (construction, soft costs) + Indirect (admin, monitoring). | Budget template (Section 5.1). |
| **2. Quantify Benefits** | Economic (jobs, tax revenue), Social (housing units, reduced crime). | IMPLAN, RIMS II, or simple multiplier tables. |
| **3. Discount Rate** | Use 3 % real discount rate (HUD guidance). | Excel NPV function. |
| **4. Net Present Value (NPV)** | Benefits – Costs; NPV > 0 indicates favorable. | Spreadsheet. |
| **5. Sensitivity Analysis** | Vary key assumptions (vacancy rate, wage growth). | Data tables. |

**Sample Result (Town A):**  

- **Total Project Cost:** $2,000,000  
- **Estimated Annual Economic Impact:** $800,000 (jobs, local procurement)  
- **NPV (10‑yr horizon):** $1.2 M → **Benefit‑Cost Ratio (BCR) = 1.6**  

HUD reviewers look for **BCR ≥ 1.0** and clear articulation of LMI benefits.  

### 3.3. Stakeholder Engagement & Community Buy‑In  

| Stakeholder | Engagement Method | Frequency | Output |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| Residents (LMI) | Door‑to‑door canvass, town‑hall meetings | Monthly (first 3 months) | Survey results, letters of support |
| Local Business Association | Round‑table workshops | Bi‑monthly | Market‑study input, partnership agreements |
| Non‑profits (Housing, Workforce) | Joint grant‑writing sessions | Quarterly | In‑kind service commitments |
| Regional Planning Agency | Technical assistance | As needed | GIS data, transportation coordination |
| State HUD Office | Pre‑submission conference call | Once (mid‑process) | Feedback on eligibility, alignment |

**Best Practice:** Produce a **Stakeholder Engagement Log** (date, participant, key takeaways, action items). Attach a **summary** to the application.

### 5.3. Checklist of Common Pitfalls  

| # | Pitfall | How to Avoid |
|---|---------|--------------|
| 1 | **Insufficient LMI focus** – <70 % of funds not clearly tied to LMI. | Use disaggregated data; attach a “LMI Beneficiary Matrix.” |
| 2 | **Weak Matching Documentation** – vague letters, no valuation. | Provide signed, notarized letters with detailed scope and fair‑market value calculations. |
| 3 | **Over‑ambitious Scope** – projects exceed staff capacity. | Phase projects; start with a “pilot” component. |
| 4 | **Missing Certifications** – HUD Form 4220, 4220‑A not signed. | Create a pre‑submission checklist; have the City Attorney review. |
| 5 | **Late Submission** – portal closes before final edits. | Submit **one week early**; keep a “submission buffer.” |
| 6 | **Inadequate Cost‑Benefit Analysis** – no quantifiable benefits. | Use IMPLAN or a simple multiplier; include sensitivity tables. |
| 7 | **Poor Stakeholder Engagement** – no letters of support. | Log all meetings; gather at least 5–7 letters from diverse partners. |
| 8 | **Failure to Align with HUD Priorities** – e.g., climate resilience ignored. | Reference the latest HUD guidance; incorporate green infrastructure if possible. |
| 9 | **Unclear Sustainability Plan** – fund runs out after grant period. | Identify revenue streams (leasing, user fees) and a post‑grant maintenance plan. |
|10 | **Inadequate Reporting System** – cannot track outcomes. | Set up a simple database (Excel or cloud‑based) before project start. |

## 6. Measuring Long‑Term Impact & Reporting  

### 6.1. Performance Metrics & Indicators  

| Category | Indicator | Target (example) | Data Source |
|----------|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| **Housing** | # of LMI housing units created/rehab | 12 units/yr | HUD‑I, local housing authority |
| **Economic** | Jobs created (direct & indirect) | 30 jobs/yr | Payroll records, BLS |
| **Public Services** | Miles of streets/utility upgraded | 2 mi/yr | Public Works reports |
| **Business** | New businesses launched in incubator | 8/yr | Business licenses |
| **Environmental** | % reduction in energy use (kWh) | 15 % vs. baseline | Utility bills |
| **Community** | Resident satisfaction (survey) | ≥ 80 % favorable | Annual community survey |

**Reporting Frequency:**  

- **Quarterly Progress Report** (HUD Form 4220‑B) – narrative + financial updates.  
- **Annual Performance Report** – includes all metrics, photos, and a brief impact story.  
- **Final Close‑Out Report** (within 90 days of project end) – full accounting, lessons learned, and sustainability plan.

### 6.3. Sustaining Benefits After Funding Ends  

1. **Create a “Maintenance Fund”** – allocate a percentage (e.g., 2 % of annual municipal budget) to upkeep infrastructure.  
2. **Leverage Private‑Sector Partnerships** – lease incubator space at market‑rate after the first 3 years; reinvest proceeds.  
3. **Embed Programs in Existing Agencies** – move business‑development services to the Economic Development Corp. for continuity.  
4. **Seek Follow‑On Funding** – apply for USDA Rural Development, State Economic Revitalization, or private foundation grants using the CDBG project as a proven model.
