**Grant Compliance and Acquittals: Ensuring Accountability, Avoiding Pitfalls**  
**Introduction**  
Grants are critical lifelines for nonprofits, academic institutions, and community organizations striving to address societal challenges. However, securing funding comes with rigorous compliance requirements to ensure ethical and effective use of resources. ### **What Are Acquittals?**  
**Definition and Purpose**  
An *acquittal* is a formal statement certifying that a grant recipient has spent funds in compliance with the donor’s terms and applicable laws. Issued by auditors or compliance officers, acquittals confirm that activities and financial records align with the grant agreement. For example, a university receiving a NIH grant must produce an acquittal report detailing how funds supported specific research projects, staff salaries, and equipment purchases.  

**Key Components**  
1. **Financial Accuracy**: Reconciliation of expenditures with approved budgets.  
2. **Narrative Reporting**: Descriptions of how funds advanced the project’s goals.  
3. **Legal Compliance**: Adherence to federal, state, and donor-specific regulations.  
4. **Timeliness**: Submission within deadlines (e.g., 60–90 days post-project).

### **Common Requirements Across Funders**  
**Government vs. Philanthropic Standards**  
While requirements vary, both funders emphasize transparency. For instance:  
- **Government Grants (e.g., NSF, CDC)**: Mandate adherence to the Single Audit Act, which requires annual audits for organizations receiving $750,000+ in federal funds. The CDC may demand detailed progress reports on public health outcomes.  
- **Philanthropic Grants (e.g., Gates Foundation, Ford Foundation)**: Often prioritize impact narratives and alignment with mission-driven goals. The Gates Foundation might require quarterly updates on malaria vaccination campaigns.  

**Universal Requirements**  
- **Budget Compliance**: Staying within approved budgets and justifying deviations.  
- **Documentation**: Retaining receipts, invoices, and contracts for at least three to seven years.  
- **Conflict of Interest Disclosures**: Reporting personal or financial ties that could influence decisions.

**Audit Types**  
1. **Compliance Audits**: Focus on adherence to grant terms.  
2. **Financial Statement Audits**: Assess overall fiscal health.

### **Avoiding Common Compliance Failures**  
**1. Poor Record-Keeping**  
**Risk**: In the 2022 *Smith v. Green Foundation* case, a nonprofit lost $500K in grants for failing to retain donor correspondence.  
**Solution**: Digitize records, train staff, and use automated retention schedules.  

**2. Budget Mismanagement**  
**Risk**: A 2021 CDC audit found 15% of grantees overspent on administrative costs, diverting funds from program needs.  
**Solution**: Use zero-based budgeting and monthly variance analyses.  

**3. Unreported Subawards**  
**Risk**: A university lost $750K in NIH funding after failing to disclose a subcontractor’s conflict of interest.  
**Solution**: Implement a subaward management system with pre-approval workflows.  

**4. Misuse of Funds**  
**Risk**: In 2017, a nonprofit faced legal action for using grant funds to lobby, violating the Johnson Amendment.  
**Solution**: Establish clear spending policies and designate compliance officers.  

**5. Missed Deadlines**  
**Risk**: A 2020 study showed 20% of grantees faced penalties for late acquittal submissions.  
**Solution**: Use project management software with automated reminders.
