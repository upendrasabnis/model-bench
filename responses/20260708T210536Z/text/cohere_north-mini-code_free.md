# Best Practices in Nonprofit Grant Writing

## 1. Understanding the Grant Landscape  

1. **Identify Funding Types**  
   - **Program‑Specific Grants** – fund a particular project or service.  
   - **General Operating Support** – cover core costs (staff, rent, utilities).  
   - **Capacity‑Building Grants** – improve organization’s infrastructure (technology, training).  
   - **Endowment or Recurring Grants** – provide multi‑year or permanent funding.  

2. **Determine Eligibility**  
   - Review each funder’s **Mission Alignment**, **Geographic Focus**, **Funding Amount Range**, **Applicant Type** (e.g., 501(c)(3), community groups, schools).  
   - Note **Deadline Frequency** (annual, quarterly, rolling).  

3. **Create a Funding Database**  
   - Use a spreadsheet with columns: Funder Name, Website, Funding Focus, Eligibility, Deadline, Award Size, Application Requirements, Contact Person, Notes.  
   - Update monthly; flag upcoming deadlines in bold.  

4. **Set Priorities**  
   - Align grant opportunities with your **strategic plan** and **annual goals**.  
   - Rank opportunities by **fit**, **potential impact**, and **resource intensity**.  

5. **Check for Restrictions**  
   - **Geographic** – e.g., “funds only projects in the Midwest.”  
   - **Demographic** – e.g., “serves youth ages 5‑12.”  
   - **Matching Requirements** – e.g., “requires 20% in-kind contribution.”

## 2. Developing a Research Checklist  

- [ ] Verify funder’s website for the latest guidelines.  
- [ ] Download the full RFP or grant application.  
- [ ] Note the **application deadline** and any **submission portals**.  
- [ ] Record **required documents** (budget, logic model, letters of support).  
- [ ] Identify **contact person** for clarification questions.  
- [ ] Flag any **special instructions** (e.g., “no fax,” “use specific format”).  
- [ ] Note **funding history** of the organization (previous grants, success rate).  
- [ ] Document **similar past projects** that received funding.

## 3. Crafting a Compelling Needs Statement  

1. **Start with Data**  
   - Use **statistics** from reputable sources (census, health departments, local schools).  
   - Example: “In 2023, the city’s poverty rate was 18%, up from 14% in 2018.”  

2. **Tell a Human Story**  
   - Include a brief **case study**: name, age, situation, desired outcome.  
   - Example: “Maria, a 7‑year‑old with autism, struggles to communicate in her public school. Without speech therapy, she risks falling behind academically.”  

3. **Explain the Gap**  
   - Clearly state **what currently exists** vs. **what is needed**.  
   - Example: “Current community centers offer after‑school tutoring, but none provide specialized math enrichment for gifted students.”  

4. **Link to the Funder’s Mission**  
   - Show how the need **aligns** with the funder’s priorities.  
   - Example: “Your foundation’s focus on ‘education innovation’ directly addresses this gap.”  

5. **Be Specific and Measurable**  
   - Define **target population**, **geographic area**, **scale**, and **expected outcomes**.  
   - Example: “Provide math enrichment to 30 gifted students ages 8‑12 in Neighborhood X for the 2025 school year.”  

6. **Use Clear, Concise Language**  
   - Avoid jargon; write at a **8th‑grade reading level**.  
   - Limit the needs statement to **150–250 words**.  

---

## 4. Writing the Needs Statement – Step‑by‑Step  

1. **Draft the Context** – background data and trends.  
2. **Add the Story** – personal narrative to humanize statistics.  
3. **Identify the Problem** – gap analysis.  
4. **Tie to Funder** – align with their mission.  
5. **Define the Solution** – brief description of the proposed program.  
6. **Revise** – read aloud, ask peers for feedback, check length.

## 5. Example Needs Statement  

> “In our city of 250,000 residents, 1 in 5 children lives in households with incomes below the federal poverty line. Despite a 30% increase in after‑school program enrollment over the past five years, only 10% of low‑income students receive STEM enrichment. Without targeted intervention, these students risk falling 2‑3 grade levels behind their peers by high school. XYZ Foundation’s commitment to ‘equitable STEM education’ directly addresses this shortfall. We propose a pilot after‑school STEM club serving 40 students ages 8‑12 in three under‑served schools, delivering weekly hands‑on workshops led by volunteer engineers. This program will increase participants’ STEM confidence scores by at least 20% within one year, aligning with your foundation’s goal of expanding STEM access in underserved communities.”

## 6. Developing the Budget  

1. **Use a Two‑Column Format**  
   - **Line Item** | **Amount**  
   - Include **Personnel**, **Travel**, **Supplies**, **Equipment**, **Indirect Costs**, **In‑Kind Contributions**.  

2. **Separate Fixed and Variable Costs**  
   - Fixed: rent, salaries (full‑time staff).  
   - Variable: supplies, per‑student materials.  

3. **Include Realistic Assumptions**  
   - Example: “Salaries based on 0.5 FTE for 6 months,” “Travel at $0.55 per mile.”  

4. **Add a Narrative Explanation**  
   - Justify each major line item; show how costs map to project activities.  

5. **Use Consistent Formatting**  
   - Align all dollar amounts to the same decimal place (e.g., $1,250.00).  

6. **Include In‑Kind Contributions**  
   - List volunteer hours, donated materials, facility use, etc., with a monetary value.  

---

## 7. Budget Checklist  

- [ ] All expenses are **necessary** for the project.  
- [ ] Costs are **reasonable** and **market‑driven** (use local quotes).  
- [ ] Salaries include **benefits** and **indirect costs** if applicable.  
- [ ] Travel costs include **per diem** and **mileage**.  
- [ ] Supplies have **unit costs** and **quantities**.  
- [ ] Equipment includes **purchase vs. lease** justification.  
- [ ] In‑kind contributions are **documented** with supporting letters.  
- [ ] Budget totals **match** the requested amount in the RFP.  
- [ ] Budget is **clear** and **easy to read** (fonts, spacing).

## 9. Building a Logic Model  

1. **Define Inputs**  
   - Funding, staff, facilities, materials, partnerships.  

2. **List Activities**  
   - What you will do (e.g., “deliver 12 weekly STEM workshops”).  

3. **Identify Outputs**  
   - Tangible products (e.g., “20 students complete a science project”).  

4. **Determine Outcomes**  
   - Short‑term (knowledge), intermediate (confidence), long‑term (career interest).  

5. **Show Assumptions**  
   - External factors that must hold (e.g., “volunteers available”).  

6. **Create a Visual Diagram**  
   - Use boxes and arrows: Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes.  

---

## 10. Logic Model Checklist  

- [ ] **Inputs** are realistic and documented.  
- [ ] **Activities** are specific, measurable, and time‑bound.  
- [ ] **Outputs** are quantifiable (e.g., number of participants).  
- [ ] **Outcomes** align with funder’s strategic goals.  
- [ ] **Assumptions** are noted and risk‑mitigated.  
- [ ] The model is **visual** and easy to scan.

## 11. Writing the Proposal Narrative  

1. **Executive Summary (1 page)**  
   - Summarize problem, solution, budget, and impact.  

2. **Organization Profile**  
   - History, mission, values, staff, past achievements.  

3. **Problem Statement**  
   - Re‑state needs statement with data.  

4. **Program Description**  
   - Detailed description of activities, target population, implementation timeline.  

5. **Evaluation Plan**  
   - Include logic model, metrics, data collection methods, reporting schedule.  

6. **Budget Narrative**  
   - Explain each line item, justify amounts, and show alignment with activities.  

7. **Sustainability**  
   - How the program will continue after grant ends (e.g., community ownership, revenue streams).  

8. **Letters of Support**  
   - Include at least 3–5 letters from partners, community leaders, or beneficiaries.

## 12. Common Pitfalls to Avoid  

- **Vague Goals** – “improve education” vs. “increase math proficiency by 20%”.  
- **Over‑Budgeting** – ask for more than realistic.  
- **Missing Deadlines** – always set internal deadlines 1–2 weeks before funder deadlines.  
- **Poor Formatting** – use consistent fonts, headings, and numbering.  
- **Lack of Alignment** – ensure every section directly addresses funder’s criteria.  
- **Inadequate Evaluation** – provide clear metrics and reporting plan.

## 13. Post‑Award Management  

1. **Create a Project Timeline**  
   - Break down milestones: kickoff, activity phases, evaluation data collection, final report due.  

2. **Set Up a Monitoring System**  
   - Use a spreadsheet to track progress, expenditures, and deliverables.  

3. **Maintain Communication**  
   - Send **monthly updates** to the funder (brief, positive).  
   - Respond promptly to **requests for information (RFIs)**.  

4. **Document Everything**  
   - Keep copies of all receipts, contracts, correspondence, and participant records.  

5. **Conduct Mid‑Term Review**  
   - Compare actual outcomes to projected outcomes; adjust activities if needed.  

6. **Prepare Final Report**  
   - Follow funder’s report format (often PDF, narrative + financial).  
   - Include **photos, testimonials, and data visualizations**.  

7. **Submit Financial Close‑out**  
   - Finalize budget reconciliation, return any unused funds, and provide a final accounting.  

---

## 14. Post‑Award Reporting Checklist  

- [ ] Review funder’s **report guidelines** (length, format, required attachments).  
- [ ] Gather **program data** (surveys, test scores, attendance logs).  
- [ ] Compile **financial statements** (actual vs. budget).  
- [ ] Write a **narrative summary** covering: introduction, objectives, methods, results, lessons learned.  
- [ ] Include **visuals** (charts, photos, infographics) to enhance readability.  
- [ ] Obtain **sign‑off** from senior staff and board.  
- [ ] Submit the report **on time** and keep a record of submission confirmation.

## 16. Building Relationships for Future Funding  

1. **Maintain a Funder Relations Log**  
   - Record contacts, meeting dates, grant outcomes, and follow‑up actions.  

2. **Send Thank‑You Notes**  
   - Within 48 hours of award notification; personalize with specific project references.  

3. **Invite to Events**  
   - Offer funder representatives a tour or invitation to program activities.  

4. **Share Impact Stories**  
   - Quarterly newsletters highlighting beneficiary outcomes.  

5. **Seek Feedback**  
   - Ask funders what they liked and what could be improved for future applications.

## 17. Continuous Improvement Checklist  

- [ ] Review past grant performance against proposed outcomes.  
- [ ] Identify **successful elements** and **problematic areas**.  
- [ ] Update **standard operating procedures** (SOPs) accordingly.  
- [ ] Train new staff on **grant writing best practices**.  
- [ ] Refine **budget templates** and **logic model formats**.  
- [ ] Expand **network** of partners and volunteers.  

---

## 18. Final Thoughts  

- **Research thoroughly** – every funder has nuanced priorities.  
- **Tell a compelling story** – data + human narrative = persuasive.  
- **Budget realistically** – detail each line and justify.  
- **Model impact** – logic model is your roadmap to success.  
- **Report transparently** – build trust for future funding.  
- **Iterate and improve** – each grant refines your capacity.
