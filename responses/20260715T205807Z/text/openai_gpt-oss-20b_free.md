## 1. What Is Grant Acquittal Reporting?

Acquittal reporting, also known as final reporting, closure reporting, or grant closing, is the formal process by which a grantee confirms that:

1. All grant activities have been completed (or paused in a clearly documented way).
2. All financial obligations have been settled.
3. All deliverables have been submitted or are in the process of being submitted.
4. All reporting requirements have been met, including financial statements, progress reports, and any specific outcome or impact data.

In short, it is the “I’m done” statement that allows the funder to close the account, release any remaining funds, and move on to the next grant.

## 5. How to Avoid the Most Frequent Compliance Failures

Below are the five most common compliance pitfalls and actionable steps to avoid them.

### 5.1 Failure to Track Expenditures in Real Time

**Problem**: Expenditures exceed the budget because they are not tracked in a timely manner.

**Solution**: 
1. Use a dedicated grant accounting system with real‑time dashboards.
2. Assign a “Grant Officer” responsible for daily expense entry.
3. Set up alerts for any expense that exceeds 80% of the budgeted amount for a given category.

### 5 secund 2: Inadequate Documentation

**Solution**:

3. Keep a backup in a separate cloud folder.

### 5.3 Misclassifying Direct vs. Indirect Costs

**Problem**: Over‑reporting direct costs leads to inflated figures that trigger audits.

**Solution**:
1. Train staff on the definition of indirect costs (overhead, fringe benefits).
2. Automate cost allocation using a cost‑allocation matrix.
3. Perform quarterly reviews of cost classification.

### 5.4 Ignoring Reporting Deadlines

**Problem**: Late reports can result in penalties or loss of future funding.

**Solution**:
1. Build a master calendar withcion for each funder’s deadlines.
2. Automate reminders 30, 15, and 5 days before each deadline.
3. Assign a “Reporting Lead” to ensure all deliverables are ready.

### 5.5 Not Conducting a Final Self‑Audit

**Problem**: The final report contains errors that compromise acquittal.

**Solution**:
1. Perform a “pre‑acquittal” audit: reconcile all accounts, verify deliverables, and cross‑check the final report against the grant plan.
2. Involve an independent reviewer (e.g., a colleague from another department).
3. Use a standardized “Acquittal Checklist” (see Section 7).
