Below you will find concise responses to each of the five items you raised. These points are drawn from the standard post‑award audit framework that the funding body applies to all grants of this nature, and they should help you plan the engagement of an appropriate audit provider well in advance of the final disbursement.

1. **Scope of the audit** – The audit is **both financial and performance‑based**. While the core focus is a verification of expenditures against the approved budget, the auditor is also required to assess the accuracy of the technical performance data that you submit in your quarterly reports (e.g., kilowatt‑hours generated, system uptime, and any other metrics stipulated in the grant agreement). In practice, the auditor will reconcile the financial ledger with the operational outputs to confirm that the resources allocated are delivering the promised renewable‑energy outcomes.

2. **Auditor qualifications** – The funding body accepts **any independent auditor who meets the standard criteria for audit independence and professional competence**, provided that the firm holds a current practising certificate in the jurisdiction where the audit will be performed. While accreditation with a UK professional body such as the ICAEW or ACCA is **strongly recommended** (and may simplify the review process), it is **not a mandatory requirement**. The key conditions are: (a) the auditor must be able to issue a report in accordance with generally accepted auditing standards, and (b) the auditor must be free from any conflict of interest with Green Horizons Initiative or the awarding agency.

3. **Timing and submission** – The 90‑day deadline for the final audit report **begins on the date the final technical report is formally submitted to the grant manager**. This is the point at which the project’s technical deliverables are considered complete, and the audit clock starts thereafter. If, however, the final technical report is submitted after the last physical activity on site, the 90‑day period still counts from the submission date of that report, not from the completion of the last activity.

4. **Documentation package** – In addition to the audited financial statements, the audit submission must include **comprehensive supporting schedules** that detail: (a) payroll records for all personnel funded under the grant, (b) original procurement invoices and contracts for goods and services, and (c) sub‑contractor agreements together with evidence of payment flow. A simple summary of line items will not satisfy the documentation requirement; the auditor must be able to trace each expense back to its source document. Electronic copies of these schedules are acceptable, provided they are clearly indexed and uploaded to the grant management portal.

5. **Electronic versus hard‑copy delivery** – The audit report **may be delivered electronically** via the grant management portal, provided that the electronic submission includes a **digitally signed PDF** of the final audit report and all accompanying schedules. A printed, signed copy is **not required** unless the funding body specifically requests it for archival purposes. Nevertheless, you should retain a hard‑copy version for your internal records, as the portal may experience temporary technical issues that could delay validation.
