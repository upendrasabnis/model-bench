# Grant Compliance and Acquittals: A Comprehensive Guide  

## Introduction  

Grant compliance is the systematic adherence to the terms, conditions, and reporting obligations that a funder imposes on a recipient in exchange for financial support. Whether the money comes from a federal agency, a state government, a local municipality, or a private foundation, compliance is the bridge that ensures public or philanthropic trust is maintained while the funded work advances scientific, social, or community goals.  

Acquittals—sometimes called “final reports” or “close‑out reports”—are a critical component of this compliance ecosystem. An acquittal formally confirms that the grantee has fulfilled all obligations, returned any surplus funds, and resolved any outstanding issues. Failure to produce an accurate acquittal can trigger audits, fund debarment, or loss of future funding.  

This article walks through what acquittal reporting entails, the common requirements across government and philanthropic funders, best practices for record‑keeping and audits, and the most frequent compliance failures—complete with real‑world examples—to help grantees avoid costly pitfalls.

## What Is Acquittal Reporting?  

### Definition  

Acquittal reporting (also called “grant close‑out” or “final reporting”) is the formal process by which a grantee demonstrates to a funder that the awarded grant has been completed in accordance with the award terms. The acquittal typically includes:  

| Component | What It Shows | Why It Matters |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| **Final Financial Report** | All expenditures, revenues, and fund balances | Confirms that money was spent according to the budget and allowable cost principles |
| **Technical Report** | Project results, deliverables, publications, patents | Demonstrates that the scientific or programmatic objectives were met |
| **Compliance Certification** | Attestation that all grant conditions were satisfied | Provides legal assurance to the funder |
| **Return of Surplus Funds** (if any) | Documentation of any unspent money returned to the funder | Ensures fiscal accountability |
| **Audit Clearance** | Evidence that any required audits have been completed and accepted | Reduces risk of future audit triggers |

### Purpose  

1. **Legal Closure** – Provides the funder with proof that the grant agreement is fulfilled, limiting liability for both parties.  
2. **Financial Reconciliation** – Aligns the funder’s records with the grantee’s final accounting.  
3. **Knowledge Transfer** – Captures lessons learned, best practices, and outcomes for future funding decisions.  
4. **Regulatory Compliance** – Meets federal regulations (e.g., OMB Uniform Guidance) and foundation policies that mandate close‑out procedures.  

### Legal Context  

- **Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200)** – Federal grants must be closed out according to the “closeout requirements” in §200.343–§200.345.  
- **Foundation Grant Agreements** – Most major foundations (e.g., Gates, Ford, Mellon) include a “final report” clause that mirrors federal requirements.  
- **State and Local Regulations** – Many jurisdictions adopt the Uniform Guidance or have their own close‑out rules.

### Government Funders

| **National Science Foundation (NSF)** | • Final project report (NPR) via Research.gov<br>• Final financial report (FFR) via NSF’s Financial Reporting System<br>• Data Management Plan compliance<br>• Return of equipment (if required) | 180 days after award end |

**Common Themes:**  

- **Electronic portals** (eRA Commons, Research.gov, Grants.gov) are mandatory for submission.  
- **Standard forms** (SF‑425, FFR, RPPR) must be completed with exact data elements.  
- **Publication acknowledgments** are required; failure to include the funder’s acknowledgment can delay future funding.  
- **Surplus fund return** is expected unless the funder has approved a carry‑forward.

**Common Themes:**

- **Independent verification** (audit or third‑party evaluation) is often required for larger grants.  
- **Public dissemination** requirements (e.g., public webinars, open‑access publications).  
- **Flexibility** in format but strict adherence to the funder’s template and word limits.

## Record‑Keeping: The Foundation of Compliance  

### Financial Records  

- **General Ledger** – Must reflect all grant‑related transactions, with clear cost codes and object codes.  
- **Bank Reconciliations** – Monthly statements with explanations for any differences.  
- **Receipts & Invoices** – Original documentation for every expenditure, retained for at least **7 years** (Uniform Guidance requirement).  
- **Budget vs. Actual Reports** – Quarterly comparisons stored in a central repository (e.g., SharePoint, DocuShare).  

**Real‑World Example:**  
The University of California, San Francisco (UCSF) was cited in a 2018 NIH audit for missing receipts on a $45,000 equipment purchase. The audit required the university to repay the amount and imposed an additional compliance training requirement. After implementing a mandatory receipt‑scan workflow, UCSF reduced audit findings by 85 % over two years.  

### Personnel and Time Sheets  

- **Personnel Activity Reports (PARs)** – Must be signed by the principal investigator (PI) and supervisor, detailing time spent on the grant.  
- **Budgeted vs. Actual FTE** – Track any deviations and obtain funder approval before rebudgeting.  
- **Travel & Conference Attendance** – Detailed itineraries, registration fees, and per‑diem logs.  

### Procurement and Contracts  

- **Procurement Approvals** – Document the three‑tier approval process (department head, finance, and, for items >$5,000, a procurement committee).  
- **Contract Agreements** – Keep the original signed contract, any amendments, and the “no‑cost‑extension” approvals.  
- **Vendor Invoices** – Match invoices to purchase orders and receiving reports.  

**Key Regulation:** The Uniform Guidance’s “Cost Principles” (2 CFR Part 200, Subpart E) define allowable costs and require that all procurement transactions be supported by written documentation.  

### Subaward Reporting  

- **Subaward Agreement** – Include scope, budget, reporting requirements, and audit provisions.  
- **Subawardee Certifications** – Ensure subawardees provide a *Certifying Officer’s* statement that they are not suspended or debarred.  
- **Reporting** – Submit the subawardee’s final report to the prime grantee within the same deadline as the prime grant’s acquittal.  

**Real‑World Example:**  
In 2020, the University of Kentucky was fined $250,000 by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) for failing to monitor a subawardee’s compliance with federal cost principles. The university later instituted a subaward monitoring checklist that is reviewed quarterly, eliminating similar findings in subsequent audits.

## Audits: Navigating Internal and External Reviews  

### Types of Audits  

| Audit Type | Who Conducts It | Primary Focus |
|------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Financial Audit** | Independent CPA firms (or internal audit offices) | Accuracy of financial statements, compliance with GAAP and cost principles |
| **Compliance Audit** | Federal agencies (e.g., HHS, NSF) or foundation compliance officers | Adherence to grant terms, reporting, procurement, and subaward requirements |
| **Programmatic Audit** | Funders’ program staff or external evaluators | Achievement of outcomes, deliverables, and impact metrics |
| **Internal Audit** | Institution’s Office of Internal Audit | Controls, risk management, and policy effectiveness |

### Frequency and Triggers  

- **Federal grants** typically require an **annual financial audit** if the grant is over the $750k threshold (Uniform Guidance). Smaller grants may be audited only if red flags appear.  
- **Foundations** often request a **single audit** for the life of the grant, but many require an independent review for grants >$500k.  
- **Triggers** for unscheduled audits include:  
  - Late or missing acquittal reports.  
  - Significant cost overruns.  
  - Reports of fraud or mismanagement.  
  - Changes in key personnel without proper handoff.  

### Preparation Steps  

1. **Gather Documentation** – Compile all required files (financial statements, PARs, procurement approvals, subaward agreements). Use a checklist aligned with the funder’s close‑out requirements.  
2. **Reconcile Accounts** – Ensure the grant’s general ledger matches the final financial report.  
3. **Conduct a Pre‑Audit Review** – Perform an internal “gap analysis” to identify potential deficiencies before the external auditor arrives.  
4. **Train Staff** – Ensure the grant team understands the audit process, the scope, and how to respond to auditor inquiries.  
5. **Engage the Compliance Officer** – The compliance officer should be the point of contact for auditors and should sign off on the final audit response.  

**Real‑World Example:**  
The Stanford University School of Medicine faced a 2019 NSF compliance audit after a random review flagged a $120,000 equipment purchase lacking a vendor invoice. The university’s internal audit team identified the missing documentation two weeks before the external audit, allowing them to reconstruct the paper trail and provide a complete explanation. The external auditors concluded with a “clean” opinion, and the university avoided a $50,000 penalty.

## Frequent Compliance Failures and How to Avoid Them  

### 1. Failure to Submit Timely Acquittal Reports  

- **Why It Happens:** Overlooking deadlines, missing required electronic portals, or awaiting final data collection that never materializes.  
- **Consequences:** Late fees, suspension of future funding, and audit triggers.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Create a **grant calendar** with hard deadlines for each reporting milestone.  
  - Use **automated reminders** (e.g., Grants.gov alerts, institutional reminder systems).  
  - Designate a **grant manager** responsible for tracking all close‑out tasks.  

**Real‑World Example:**  
The University of Washington’s Department of Chemistry missed the NIH acquittal deadline for a $2.5 M grant by 45 days. The NIH imposed a $15,000 late‑fee and placed the grant on “high risk” status, leading to increased scrutiny for the next three years. After implementing a centralized grant compliance dashboard, the department reduced acquittal delays to under 5 days for all subsequent awards.  

### 2. Inaccurate Cost Allocation  

- **Why It Happens:** Misclassifying indirect costs, failing to allocate salary costs correctly, or using outdated cost allocation bases.  
- **Consequences:** Overpayment recovery, audit findings, and potential debarment.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Conduct **monthly cost allocation reviews**.  
  - Use **validated cost accounting software** that automatically applies the approved allocation methodology.  
  - Maintain **up‑to‑date indirect rate agreements** with the funder.  

### 3. Lack of Proper Documentation for Procurement  

- **Why It Happens:** Assuming small purchases (<$5,000) are exempt from formal procurement policies.  
- **Consequences:** Audit findings for non‑compliant purchases, disallowed costs, and required repayment.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Apply **uniform procurement policies** to all purchases, regardless of amount.  
  - Use a **standardized purchase requisition form** that captures all required approvals.  
  - Store **electronic copies** of all procurement documents in a searchable repository.  

### 4. Misuse of Funds (e.g., Personal Expenses)  

- **Why It Happens:** Unintentional commingling of grant funds with institutional accounts or using grant money for personal travel.  
- **Consequences:** Criminal fraud investigations, repayment of misused funds, and loss of sponsorship.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Implement **segregated bank accounts** for grant funds where required.  
  - Provide **training on allowable vs. unallowable expenses** for all grant personnel.  
  - Conduct **random spot checks** of expense reports.  

### 5. Failure to Meet Reporting Deadlines (Progress, Financial)  

- **Why It Happens:** Overloading of research responsibilities, staff turnover, or lack of clear ownership.  
- **Consequences:** Funders may deem the project “non‑performing,” leading to suspension or termination.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Adopt a **dual‑track reporting system**: one for internal tracking, another for external submission.  
  - Use **template-based reporting** to reduce manual entry errors.  
  - Schedule **quarterly check‑ins** with the funder’s program officer to confirm alignment.  

### 6. Subaward Non‑Compliance  

- **Why It Happens:** Assuming subawardees handle their own compliance, leading to gaps in monitoring.  
- **Consequences:** Prime grantee held liable for subawardee violations, monetary penalties, and debarment risk.  
- **Prevention:**  
  - Draft **comprehensive subaward agreements** that mirror the prime grant’s compliance requirements.  
  - Perform **annual subaward compliance reviews** (including audits if required).  
  - Maintain a **subaward management log** tracking milestones, reports, and issues.

## Best Practices for Ongoing Compliance  

### 1. Designate a Compliance Officer  

- The compliance officer should be **independent of the research team** to avoid conflicts of interest.  
- Responsibilities include:  
  - Overseeing all grant agreements and amendments.  
  - Conducting **pre‑award compliance checks** (e.g., verifying that the institution is authorized to receive the specific type of funding).  
  - Coordinating **audit responses** and ensuring corrective actions are implemented.  

### 2. Develop and Update Policies  

- Create **institution‑wide grant compliance manuals** that incorporate the latest Uniform Guidance updates and funder‑specific guidelines.  
- Require **annual acknowledgment** of the policy by all grant personnel.  

### 3. Continuous Monitoring  

- Use **real‑time dashboards** that pull data from the general ledger, time‑keeping systems, and procurement software.  
- Set **automated alerts** for:  
  - Budget overruns >10 % without prior approval.  
  - Unsubmitted reports approaching their due date.  
  - Subaward reports overdue by more than 15 days.  

### 4. Training and Communication  

- Conduct **quarterly compliance workshops** covering topics such as cost allocation, audit preparation, and ethical use of funds.  
- Establish a **grant‑specific newsletter** that shares recent funder guidance, audit findings from peer institutions, and best practices.  

### 5. Foster Strong Funder Relationships  

- Schedule **mid‑project reviews** with program officers to discuss progress, potential issues, and any needed adjustments.  
- Promptly respond to **funder inquiries**—delays in response can be interpreted as non‑cooperation.
