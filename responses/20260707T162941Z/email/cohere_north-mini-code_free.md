**1. Eligibility of unincorporated, council‑auspiced groups**  
Our grant guidelines state that applicant organizations must be either registered charities, incorporated not‑for‑profit entities, or “eligible unincorporated groups” that are formally auspiced by a public‑sector body. An unincorporated group that operates under the umbrella of a local council—and for which the council holds the necessary insurances, holds the assets, and can sign the grant agreement—does qualify as an eligible applicant, provided that the council provides a written statement confirming its auspice arrangement. This statement should detail the council’s responsibility for financial oversight, risk management, and compliance with grant conditions. If you have not yet secured such a statement, I recommend requesting one from the council’s community‑services department; the document can be included with your application package.  

**2. Minimum and maximum grant amounts**  
The Community Resilience Grant does not impose a strict minimum or maximum award. Instead, the funding level is determined by the scope and cost of the proposed activities, the demonstrated community impact, and the overall available budget for the round. Typical grants for infrastructure projects such as those you describe range from $15,000 to $75,000, but applications that justify higher costs or demonstrate broader community benefit may be considered for larger awards. The key is to align the requested amount with the actual project costs and to show a clear return on investment for the community.  

**3. Eligibility of the proposed infrastructure**  
Yes, the installation of rainwater tanks and a shade structure falls squarely within the grant’s eligible cost categories. The program supports “environmental resilience and climate‑adaptation projects,” and both components directly address the challenges of increased summer temperatures and water security. Eligible costs include the purchase and installation of the tanks, associated plumbing and filtration systems, and the structural components of the shade pavilion (including framing, covering material, and anchoring). Please note that costs for ongoing maintenance, consumables (such as filters), and routine operational expenses are not considered eligible.  

**4. Recommended supporting documentation**  
To build a compelling application, we suggest you compile the following materials:  

* **Council Auspice Letter** – a formal letter from the council confirming that it will act as the fiscal host, hold insurance, and sign the grant agreement on your group’s behalf.  
* **Project Plan** – a detailed timeline (including milestones for procurement, installation, and commissioning) and a clear description of how the rainwater tanks and shade structure will achieve your resilience goals.  
* **Budget Breakdown** – a line‑item spreadsheet itemizing all capital costs (equipment, labor, permits, contingency) and clearly marking which items are eligible grant expenses.  
* **Site Plans / Drawings** – scaled diagrams showing the proposed locations of the tanks and shade structure, together with any necessary site‑access considerations.  
* **Community Impact Statement** – evidence of local need (e.g., heat‑wave statistics, usage data from the produce stall) and a description of how the project will benefit multiple community groups (e.g., garden members, school groups, seniors).  
* **Financial Statements** – the most recent audited or reviewed financial statements (or, for a small operation, a simplified profit‑and‑loss statement and cash‑flow summary) demonstrating your $40,000 turnover and the sources of income.  
* **Insurance Certificate** – the council’s certificate of insurance confirming that it covers public liability and any necessary professional liability for the project.  
* **Letters of Support** – endorsements from the council, local businesses, or community organizations that highlight the project’s relevance and sustainability.  

We also advise that you carefully read the full grant guidelines (available on our website) for any additional requirements, such as the need for an environmental impact assessment or a Indigenous engagement plan, depending on the project’s location.
