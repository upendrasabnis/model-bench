## 1. What Is an Acquittal Report?

An **acquittal**—also known as a “final report,” “close‑out report,” or “acquittal of award” in federal parlance—demonstrates that an organization has responsibly spent grant funds and met all contractual obligations. It宝典:

- **Financially**: Shows how money was expended, matching budget categories (direct, indirect, cost‑sharing).  
- **Programmatically**: Demonstrates achievement of objectives, deliverables, and measurable outcomes.  
- **Compliance‑wise**: Confirms adherence to policy restrictions, procurement rules, and reporting deadlines.

In many contexts, the term “acquittal” also refers to the *receipt* of grant funds, but this article focuses on the *final* acquittal of an award.

## 6. Audit Landscape & Compliance Checks

### 6.1 Internal Audits  

- **Purpose**: Evaluate internal controls, identify risks.  
- **Frequency**: Annual or as needed.  
- **Outcome**: Internal audit report, action plan.

### 6.2 External Audits  

- **IRS 990** (for 501(c)(3) nonprofits).  
- **OMB Circulars (A-133, A-122)** for federal grants.  
- **GAO** for large federal programs.  
- **Independent auditors** for state grants.  

### 6.3 Compliance Audits  

- **Cost‑Sharing**: Verify that cost‑sharing matches the award terms.  
- **FRA Procurement**: Check that purchases meet federal procurement thresholds and competitive bidding.  
- **Data Management**: Validate data подход compliance (e.g., NIH data sharing).  

> **Case Study**: In 2019, the **EPA** conducted a compliance audit on a Texas clean‑energy grant. The audit found that a nonprofit had not correctly documented a $120k cost‑sharing contribution; as a result, the grant was suspended for 90 days. The nonprofit instituted a new cost‑sharing tracking system, preventing recurrence.

## 7. Common Compliance Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

| Pitfall | Why it Happens | Prevention Tips |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Misuse of Restricted Funds** | Lack of clear policy, staff confusion. | • Create a “Restricted Funds” policy.<br>• Conduct quarterly reconciliation. |
| **Exceeding Indirect Cost Caps** | Over‑budgeting, misunderstanding rates. | • Use OMB rate tables.<br>• Regularly review IRR calculations. |
| **Inadequate Time & Effort Tracking** | Relying on ad‑hoc spreadsheets. | • Implement time‑tracking software (e.g., Harvest). |
| **Late Acquittal Submission** | Poor project management, late payment cycles. | • Build a submission calendar.<br>• Get reminders from the grant system. |
| **Non‑Compliance with Procurement Rules** | Using vendor without competitive bidding. | • Maintain a vendor list.<br>• Require procurement approvals. |
| **Missing Documentation for Travel** | Forgetting to submit receipts. | • Capture travel logs in real time (mobile app). |
